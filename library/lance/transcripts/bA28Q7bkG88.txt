The more you grow as a trader, inevitably the larger your negative days become. If you ask any top trader at any firm in the country who has the biggest negative days, it's probably them. That doesn't mean they're doing anything wrong. Realistically, it doesn't mean anything right. It's just not the appropriate indicator. In an ideal world, what we really want is to focus on our expected value and to obsess with this and this alone. The way I do that is the bobblehead concept. If you take a chart where on one axis it's your daily expected value and on the other axis it's time as you progress all you want to do is steadily increase your little bobblehead and imagine your head shifting higher. Maybe if you're a trainee your expected value each day might be negative 50 bucks but after a couple of months you reach that break even point where on the average day you're now a positive trader. Maybe you're approaching being consistently positive and from here you try to grow and improve not just by the number of days you're green but also the size of them. Essentially, it boils down to figuring out how you can make your time in front of that computer worth more and more with each day that goes on. Every time I talk about this concept, I try to shift the mentality from P&L to what is your expected value each day. What this does is it makes you agnostic to the actual daily performance or the opportunity set. It doesn't matter if the market was easy, if the day was considered good or bad. You want to be able to win the day based on your standards and the bar you set. So you're able to win every day regardless of what it looks
