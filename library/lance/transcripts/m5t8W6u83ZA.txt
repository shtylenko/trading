I spent five years and over $150,000 learning these five secret trading psychology rules from Dr. Jonathan Katz, a clinical psychologist that focuses his work on top hedge funds, trading firms, and traders. Oh, and he's not taking new clients, so you can't learn these rules anywhere else. Lesson one is about managing your emotions while trading, particularly rage trading or being on tilt. Once you're angry and upset, it's often already too late to calm yourself back down. Most traders undervalue lesson two, but this rule has had the biggest impact on my performance. Trading performance is holistic. You can't get in a huge fight with your spouse or get in a traffic accident before work, then think you're going to show up to work and be able to trade as if nothing happened. A calm, clear-headed mental state outside of work leads to calm, clear-headed decisions at work. That entails getting good sleep, eating well, exercise, and making sure you handle interpersonal conflict in an adaptive way so you can be focused and ready. Rule number three, the more you can focus on your own game and improving your own trading, the better off you'll be. Trading at Trillium was incredibly competitive. You were shoulderto-shoulder with over a hundred other traders fighting for the best prints, seeing everyone's scoreboard each and every day. If someone had a big win, you'd hear about it. If someone took a big loss, you'd know about that, too. And in my case, I was fighting tooth and nail to be at the top of the annual top 10 rankings. That level of competition had a lot of benefits, but it also had one big drawback. My biggest slumps have always come when I felt I was underperforming those around me on the trading floor. That comparison game crushed me until my work with Dr. Catz helped me break the cycle. And honestly, it's probably saved me millions of dollars. Rule four, our relationship with money drives a lot of who we are as a trader. No trader goes into trading with the same background, the same life experiences, or the same upbringing. As a result, not everyone has the same aspirations, and not everyone has the same relationship with money. Some traders will naturally be more conservative. They'll maximize consistency as opposed to maximizing P&L. They'll live frugally. They'll wire out their profits. Other traders will aim for the stars, live huge, and spend huge. Neither of them is wrong. A lot of our trading style is about being honest with ourselves about our goals and aspirations and making sure that our trading style reflects our desires rather than matching someone else's. Rule number five, the final rule I'll share here from Jonathan is that trading is just one part of who we are. It is okay to let it consume us at times. It is okay to be obsessed and work very hard. But to sustain a career and to find sustainable happiness, you need to have a broader identity that involves relationships, friendships, and other hobbies. Trading is a job that will always have a lot of ups and downs.
