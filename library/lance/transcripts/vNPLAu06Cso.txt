I find that the Ballinger bands prove useful in three major ways. The first is the pendulum concept. Much like a pendulum or a rubber band, stocks without fresh news can often exhibit strong mean reversion characteristics. In other words, when things go too far too fast in either direction, that rubber band tends to snap back towards the equilibrium. I'll go so far as to say that the further a stock is stretched in one direction, the higher the probability of a successful reversal and the greater in magnitude that reversal is likely to be. The second way I use Ballinger bands is as a basic measure of overextension. A stock can be considered overextended if when trending price ends up accelerating to be above or below the Ballinger bands. In my many years of experience, the absolute best capitulation plays tend to have price meaningfully extended below or above the Ballinger bands after already trending and being extended in a direction. Keep in mind, this is just one measure of overextension I consider when judging whether or not a trade is worth taking. The third way I use Ballinger bands is they help us measure volatility contraction and get a feel for if we are in the dreaded no man's land. No man's land happens when bands narrow significantly, suggesting a period of low volatility and often a precursor of a period when consolidation is needed.
