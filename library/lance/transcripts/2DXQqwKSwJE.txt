First, what is scalping and why does it work? When done correctly, scalping is a surgical process. You identify a key emotional level, confirm with the tape, and execute without hesitation. It's a very short-term trade designed to capture a small price move, often lasting seconds or minutes. It's demanding, though. There is no room for distraction, and you're interpreting real-time information and making fast, calculated decisions. The goal of scalping is to exploit micro inefficiencies in order flow, liquidity, and short-term momentum. Done effectively, you can really stack up a bunch of decent wins, and scalping is a totally viable strategy to make a trading career out of. Let me give you this example so you can picture it. An in-play stock is coiling tight against resistance at $25, but there is a large offer there. Price pushes away initially, but then it pushes up against the offer tighter and tighter. You can feel the tension and the anticipation. As the stock presses up against the large offer, boom, some buyer takes the whole offer in one shot, and everyone scrambles to buy their shares after the offer gets taken and the resistance is broken. Good scalpers are the ones either buying that offer or having a stop limit buy order just above it to buy $25 and $25.02, only then to kick it out, riding that momentum of traders who missed their entry and are scrambling to buy it higher. They might take their 5,000 shares they bought, and they're kicking it out 10 cents higher at $25.10 and $25.12. That 10 cents on 5,000 shares makes you $500, and that is a great way to make a living if you can stack these up day to day. At a high level, scalping offers several structural advantages. Positions are held for short durations, which minimizes exposure to overnight risk, headline risk, or market correlations. The strategy also produces frequent feedback, allowing traders to evaluate their performance quickly and refine their execution. In other words, scalping allows you to get tons and tons of reps, which is why this strategy is so beginner-friendly. When you get the reps, you'll develop small positive edges, which, when repeated consistently, can compound meaningfully over time. In fact, scalping was one of the very first strategies I ever learned myself. Another advantage of scalping is that the risk per trade is typically well-defined due to tight stops and limited time exposure. Unlike with swing trading, where you are exposed to overnight risk, headline risk, and the whims of the overall market, scalping is a hyper-specific bet that a specific security is going to make a small, sharp move in your favor at a very specific moment in time. However, of course, there are cons. Scalping is highly execution-dependent. Minor slippage, hesitation, or routing inefficiencies can eliminate or impair your expected value. Transaction costs matter significantly because profit targets are small relative to frequency. The strategy is also cognitively demanding, requiring sustained focus over the course of a long day. The margin for error is thin, and a single large mistake can erase many small gains. The biggest con of scalping, though, is its scalability is constrained. The more size you do, the more slippage you face, and in turn the more your edge degrades. That is why most big-time traders, like myself, eventually transition away from scalping. Scalping is not for everyone because you tend to need to be glued to the desk during market hours. To summarize what an in-play stock is, it's a stock that has a significant price range, is doing high volume, and often trading on a news catalyst or interesting technical pattern. Once you identify an in-play stock, the next step is identifying a tight level. I want a very well-defined intraday support or resistance level. Ideally, one that aligns on multiple time frames. Something other participants clearly see as well. Ultimately, the tighter the level, the more defined your risk, and that's how you can get some nice asymmetry of risk reward on these trades while also keeping up a solid win rate. Now, we get to the most important skill in scalping, reading the tape. I'd say, while the chart is 70% of the setup, reading the tape might be 30% or more of the analysis, depending on the specific trade when it comes to scalps. You need to be paying attention to how thick or thin the bids and offers are, and look for disparity as well as which side is being more aggressive. Is the offer being taken, or are the bids being smashed? The time and sales window reveals who is being more aggressive. If you are not watching the tape, which is the level two box of the buyers and sellers and the time and sales window, you are missing the story. There are three primary scalp strategies: mean reversion, continuation, and making the spread. Mean reversion scalps occur after price stretches too far too fast. You are stepping in when emotion peaks and order flow begins to shift. That shift is visible in the tape before it's obvious on the chart. On a sharp down move into support, you will often see a moment where volume stalls, and it's this pause where people stop hitting the bid. That pause is often the hesitation of sellers to push their hand and signifies a turn of momentum. As a scalper, you then take the offer and get long in front of that support level, using the bids as at the support as your protection, while then seeking to capture the bounce we get off that level. Often, these mean reversion scalps work best when the stock quickly and sharply shoots down to a support level with some massive bids or quickly and sharply shoots up to a resistance level with large offers. The sharper and bigger the move into really big bids or offers, the better it is to scalp a reversal. Continuation scalps, on the other hand, occur around breaks of clean support or resistance. When scalping here, you are trying to be the first market participant to enter upon the break. So, if we have a $10 resistance level, you want to be buying $10.01 or $10.02, and scalping out on that emotional follow-through, much like the example we gave earlier. Your success in a continuation scalp trade will depend heavily on timing and confirmation from real-time order flow. Imagine a stock that is earnings this morning. Volume is five times normal. It pushes up from $48 to $50 in the first hour. Now, it's consolidating under $50, a clean psychological resistance level. You see multiple pushes into that $50. The pullbacks are shallow, and each dip finds buyers higher than the previous dip. That is attention building. On the level two box, which is, again, the window displaying the bids and offers, you see that large offer sitting at $50. Now, you watch the tape. Are the buyers aggressively lifting into that offer? Are prints going off repeatedly at $50? Is that offer refreshing? Meaning, it's reappearing after partial fills, or is it getting taken? If buyers continue lifting and the offer size begins to shrink, then suddenly it's gone, you want to be the one buying at $50.01, if possible, and potentially kicking it out 10, 20, or even 30 cents higher. Often, your stop loss would be right below that level if it gets back below the $50. Strong breakouts feel urgent, and you want to see the stock burst through that level with conviction. Weak breakouts drift through a level and stall. The tape slows, sellers start hitting bids just above the resistance, and that often leads to failed breaks. A continuation scalp that works well will move quickly in your favor. There's also a third type of scalp that doesn't often get addressed, and that is making the spread, especially in a liquid name. Most traders think of scalping as hitting bids and lifting offers to capture short bursts of momentum. Spread making is different. Instead of aggressively taking liquidity, you provide the liquidity. You're placing resting limit orders in spread names [snorts] ahead of large bids or offers with the aim to capture the difference between the spread as other participants are crossing and hitting the bid and paying the offer. The edge here does not come from predicting direction. It comes from understanding market microstructure. When a stock is in relative equilibrium, but thin, market participants who need urgency will cross the spread. They'll pay that offer up there and hit the bid down there. In this sense, by providing that liquidity, you are acting more like a market maker than a momentum trader. To do this well, you must really understand market microstructure of order types, queue priority, and hidden versus visible orders.
