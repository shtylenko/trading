Options behave the same way. Let's say the S&P 500 is trading at $5,000. At 10:00 a.m., you buy a zero DTE call option at the $5,000 strike price for $50. Most of that $50 is time value because the option is at the money. You're paying for the possibility that the index rallies before the close. Now imagine the S&P just drifts sideways. By 1:00 p.m., it's still around $5,000. Even though price hasn't meaningfully changed, your option might now be worth only $30. Why? Because several hours of opportunity are gone. There is less time for the rally to happen. By 3:00 p.m., if the index is still flat, that option might only be worth $15 now. Not because you were wrong on direction, not because the market crashed, simply because the clock kept ticking and the move you wanted didn't happen. By 4:00 p.m., if the S&P closes at $5,000, that option expires worthless. The entire $50 is gone. That is time decay. And here's what makes it brutal with zero DTEs and how you can very quickly lose your money. Time decay accelerates into the close. In the final few hours, if the move you're betting on hasn't started, your premium can collapse quickly. That is why I said that there's no instrument that demands more selectivity than the zero DTE. You not only need to be right, but you need to be right within a specific window as well. And in the case of zero DTEs, we are talking a super narrow window, where else you can lose the full 100% of your position.
