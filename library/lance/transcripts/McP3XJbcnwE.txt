Title: One of the biggest misconceptions in trading is that longer timeframes automatically mean more edge
URL: https://youtube.com/watch?v=McP3XJbcnwE
======================================================================

One of the biggest misconceptions in trading is that longer time frames automatically mean more edge. Honestly, I think the opposite is often true. The shortest human time frames frequently contain the most edge. Now, to be clear, I personally trade more bigger picture nowadays, but that's mostly because of scalability and quality of life, not because the edge is inherently better. Here's the paradox of markets. The shortest time frames often have the biggest dislocations, the longest time frames often have the biggest tailwinds, and the middle is where many traders get chopped to pieces. Why do short time frames have so much edge? Because markets become temporarily inefficient over very small windows. Force liquidations, closing imbalances, breaking news headlines, all of these create moments where price temporarily disconnects from fair value and needs to rapidly reprice. That's why some short-term traders can have insanely high win rates and strings of hundreds of green days in a row. The edge comes from exploiting very short-lived inefficiencies before the market corrects itself. If you're prepared and fast, these can become incredibly asymmetric opportunities. But, there's a trade-off people ignore: liquidity and scalability. That's why nowadays many traders can generate absurd percentage returns on small accounts but struggle to scale. And it's also why massive funds have to gravitate towards lower return, longer-term strategies. The bigger the capital base, the more edge gets diluted. Understanding the trade-off between edge and scalability is one of the most important frameworks in trading.
