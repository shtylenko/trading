In the simplest terms possible, I view expected value as the average outcome over the long run. Let's start simple. Imagine flipping a coin. You bet $1. If it lands heads, you win $1. If it's tails, you lose a dollar. The expected value or EV is zero. 50% chance to make a dollar, 50% chance to lose one. You're not gaining or losing over time. This is a neutral EV gain. Now, let's make it interesting. Suppose you win $2 if it's heads and lose $1 if it's tails. same 50/50 coin, but now your expected value is the probability of heads or 50% time $2. The probability of tails, 50% * $1, which equals 50. In other words, you're making 50 per flip on average. You'll still have losing flips, maybe even losing streaks, but over thousands of flips, you would be printing money. Let's say you played that game 10 times. Because the expected value is positive.50, you could expect to make $5 on average because you were playing a game that on average pays you 50 10 times. Essentially, the formula works out to be expected value equals the probability of winning time the average win amount minus the probability of loss time the average loss amount. That's it. Four variables.
