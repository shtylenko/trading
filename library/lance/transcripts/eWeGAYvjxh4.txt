Selling options is dangerous, especially when you sell them naked, meaning you don't own the underlying security. But when done in a safer manner, it can be quite lucrative. Notice, I never said safe because there's always risk in trading. That's exactly why I always review my four rule checklist before selling naked options. If you violate even one of them, you are playing with fire, and don't say I didn't warn you. And don't worry, I'll walk you through some real-world examples at the end of this video to help you apply these rules to your own trading. If you're new here, I'm Lance, and I made over a hundred million dollars trading on Wall Street. But that P&L did not come without learning some extremely costly lessons. Here's the issue. Most people sell options at the exact worst times. And because of the high win rate embedded into selling options, this often lulls traders into a false sense of security. In my prior options video, which I'll link in the description, I discussed how options influencers wrongfully pitch selling options as a way of making income. I make the analogy that selling options is like writing home insurance. Every year that there isn't a catastrophe, you get more and more confident that what you are doing is genius and can't go wrong. So, what do most traders do as a result of that comfort? They get more and more aggressive in how they sell the options, implicitly taking on more and more risk at the exact worst times. And sure enough, boom, the catastrophe finally strikes. It might be a market dislocation or some huge breaking news headline, but inevitably it happens, and this is how option sellers blow up or give back all their gains they earned for weeks, months, and sometimes even years worth. Rule number one, only sell options after volatility has blown out, not in no man's land where we are range bound and volatility is calm. That analogy I gave about selling home insurance before the catastrophe is a good one, which is why the best time to sell insurance is actually right after the catastrophe when everyone else has gotten banged up. After the California wildfires in 2025, most insurers stepped away from that market. The few that remained were selling insurance at astronomical rates. That is how we want to operate in the options market. You generally want to step in and sell options after fear has spiked and implied volatility has exploded higher. You want to sell options after the big once in a decade move. It is paradoxical, but true that after the huge blowout move is actually when it is safest to do so because at sky-high implied volatility is actually offering you more risk cushion. But, if you're selling options in sleepy, compressed, range bound markets, no man's land conditions as I like to call it, when implied volatility is low, you're often collecting pennies in front of a steamroller. And eventually the steamroller arrives. So, rule one is simple. Sell options during times of extreme moves and extreme sentiment. Never, ever sell options during times of complacency. That is by far the most common mistake people make. Rule number two, only sell options at a price you'd actually want to be assigned the underlying. Before you sell calls or puts, ask yourself, "Would I genuinely be happy being long or short the underlying at this strike?" Not, "I guess I could survive it." or not, "I would hope it bounces." Actually happy and salivating over how good the opportunity would be. A lot of people trick themselves here. They tell themselves, "I wouldn't mind owning it." But, then the stock gaps down, they're underwater, and suddenly they hate the stock they supposedly wanted. That's why this cannot just be a theoretical exercise. These moves happen. Headlines happen. So, one quick trick I like to do is I graph out on the chart where the stock would be net of the premium should I be assigned the position. For example, if I sell a $100 strike put for $5, I will circle on the chart the $95 range and imagine what the move would look like for the stock to get there. Don't worry. We'll be diving into real-world examples at the end and I'll show you how I apply this visual. Rule number three, only sell options for the amount of shares you're truly willing to be assigned. If rule one is the most common mistake, then rule three is the one that actually blows traders up. A bad trade will not kill you. Being too big when you're wrong will. Losses don't come from one mistake. They come from being wrong and oversized. And with options, that risk is amplified because they don't move in a straight predictable way. Options are non-linear, which just means they don't behave like shares. If you're down 10% on stock, you're down 10%. With options, a small move in the stock can lead to a much bigger move in your position. That's because of delta, how much the option moves relative to the stock. And that delta itself changes as the trade goes against you. You might enter thinking you have a certain level of exposure, but as the stock moves, your position can become much more sensitive. You're not just losing, you're losing faster than expected. This is where traders get caught. They size the trade based on a common assumption, and I'm okay losing X amount, but they're thinking in straight lines. In reality, the position can accelerate against them. A 1 to 2% move in the stock can even turn into a 30 to 50% or more loss in the option very quickly, especially with short-gated contracts. And what once felt like a controlled risk becomes something much bigger. During periods of heightened volatility, there's another risk most traders don't factor in, margin changes. Brokers and exchanges can raise margin requirements quickly, which means the position you thought you could comfortably hold suddenly requires more capital. If you don't have it, you're forced to reduce or close the trade, often at the worst possible time. We saw this clearly during the sharp moves in gold and silver in early 2026 when the CME repeatedly increased margin requirements, forcing traders out of positions as volatility surged. Even if your idea is right longer term, you may not survive the path. Then the psychological side kicks in. When losses grow faster than expected, decision-making breaks down. Instead of following a plan, traders freeze, hope, or panic. The size of the position starts controlling them. And in fast markets, especially with options, there's often no clean exit. There's going to be spreads, liquidity can disappear, and you're going to be forced into closing the position at worst prices. That's why option sizing has to be far more conservative than most people think. The risk isn't just what you plan to lose. It's how quickly things can change and go past that. Smaller size helps you keep in control. Bigger size puts you in a position where one bad move can spiral. Over time, it's not the bad trades that matter. It's whether you were sized correctly when they happened. Here's a practical rule for you. I try to always size so that even if the option premium doubled against me and doubled against me again, I would still be okay with it. That's super conservative. And another rule of thumb, if you are losing sleep or constantly checking quotes nonstop, you are probably oversized. I tell this to every trader I mentor. Always, always, always size your naked options far more conservatively than you imagine. Selling options is all about stacking wins over time and avoiding the big whammy. Survival first, always. On to rule four. Only sell options with expirations within the window you actually want to see the move occur. Most of the options I sell tend to expire the same week, often with only even just a few days left. The further out you go on expiration, the more you expose yourself to market risk and headline risk. There are totally valid reasons to go far out with certain option expirations. For example, the leveraged ETFs tend to decay in price over time, but when you choose expiry, you're choosing your risk window. And being a short-term trader, I tend to have the strongest views about what will happen over the next day or two, not the next month or two. Additionally, short-dated out of the money options decay hard when a sharp price move doesn't occur. Essentially, your option expiration should always match the window of your thesis. Don't fall for the trap of thinking you want to go further out to collect more time value. Often that time value comes with more risk. One other bonus tip I'll sneak in here for those who are watching until the end. No matter what you trade, but particularly with options, shorting calls is infinitely more dangerous than selling puts. Because there's no lower bound of zero. There will be times when stocks make far crazier moves than you've ever imagined. Moves like we've seen in silver, the quantum computing names and more. With selling calls, you always need to be far more conservative than with selling puts. And that's even true for shorting stocks. If you take away anything from this video, remember to avoid selling options in times of no man's land, to use an appropriate strike, expiration, and contract size. Selling options is incredibly dangerous because people get complacent by their high win rate. So, no matter what, all normal risk management rules still apply. You do not want to be averaging into a losing position. You want to follow your normal loss limits, and you never want to get too concentrated. You need to always remember that the margin utilized once you get assigned can be far higher than you expect, especially to the upside. High win rate means nothing if your losers are fatal.
