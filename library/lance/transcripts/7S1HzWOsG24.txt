I'm sure you can relate to the self-doubt we all face on the journey to become a trader. I remember one pivotal conversation I had, though. I was debating whether to quit the job so I could better use my time to network and apply to other jobs. I was too afraid to have the conversation with my boss, so I spoke privately with the second highest up in our group. In our [music] conversation, he urged me to give it more time and that if I didn't, there would be a strong chance I'd end up regretting it. Why did he feel that way? At face value, I was making absolutely no progress. [music] My P&L was clearly in the red, and month after month, I was losing money. But under the [music] surface, my skill set was compounding. My ability to read the tape, manage risk, and control emotions was improving far faster than my P&L reflected. The number two in the group was able to tell from my call outs, from the questions I asked, and from the trades I was taking that I was understanding everything. And that's one of the most painful parts of this game, the lag between your competence and your results. He felt though that with more reps and more time, I would start to connect the dots better in real time. And in turn, my P&L would start to show as well. Then 3 months later was when I had my best trade of my career thus far. It was an exhaustion gap pattern in Tesla, which was something our group specialized in. I had seen this type of pattern enough times that I was able to game plan for it, recognize it in real time, and finally capitalize on it. I think that day I made something like $10,000.
