Ballinger bands are a versatile tool and truthfully pretty much the main indicator I use on my intraday and daily chart. Developed by John Ballinger in the 1980s, these bands consist of three lines. A central moving average, typically the 20 period simple moving average, and two standard deviation lines plotted above and below the moving average. From the outset, I want to make it explicitly clear that I do not use Ballinger bands as exact levels. meaning I am never buying the break above a Ballinger band. We're using a Ballinger band as a price target or anything else along those lines, pardon the pun. I find that the Ballinger bands prove useful in three major ways. The first is the pendulum concept. Much like a pendulum or a rubber band, stocks without fresh news can often exhibit strong mean reversion characteristics. In other words, when things go too far too fast in either direction, that rubber band tends to snap back towards the equilibrium. I'll go so far as to say that the further a stock is stretched in one direction, the higher the probability of a successful reversal and the greater in magnitude that reversal is likely to be. But always remember, just because a stock is stretching in one direction doesn't mean it can't always go further. During my time as a prop trader, we always had a saying, crazy can always get crazier. And economist John Maynard Keen said it best, the market can stay irrational longer than you can stay solvent. However, because Ballinger bands show standard deviations versus the equilibrium price, you can execute some interesting plays based on a stock going from steady strong to very weak or steady weak to very strong. Now, because the bands show a price two standard deviations away, the second way I use Ballinger bands is as a basic measure of overextension. A stock can be considered overextended if when trending price ends up accelerating to be above or below the Ballinger bands. In my many years of experience, the absolute best capitulation plays tend to have price meaningfully extended below or above the Ballinger bands after already trending and being extended in a direction.
