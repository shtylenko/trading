## 1. No Man's Land Trading

**Videos:**
- [Stop Trading in "No Man's Land"](https://youtube.com/watch?v=FMxPiKlZuvM) (13K views)
- [$GME No Man's Land Debate](https://youtube.com/watch?v=K-t2XreQuUM) (4.5K views)

Lance describes "No Man's Land" as the choppy range that forms after a stock makes a big directional leg, pulls back, and then starts consolidating with contracting volatility. Traders get caught trying to position themselves ahead of the next breakout and take repeated small losses ("paper cuts") that add up. He explains that the worst place to be is in the middle range where there's no clear edge — neither support nor resistance is being tested. The key rule: only trade at the edges of the range where you have a defined risk level. If a stock has made a leg up, pulled back, and is chopping sideways, wait until it either reclaims the highs (go long) or breaks below support (go short). He acknowledges exceptions — when the quality of the consolidation is exceptionally tight with good liquidity — but for most traders, staying out of No Man's Land is the single biggest improvement they can make. His framework is: identify the range edges, only trade with clear invalidation levels, and sit on your hands in between.

## 2. Right Side of the "V" Strategy

**Video:** [This Right Side of the "V" Strategy Made Me Millions](https://youtube.com/watch?v=wtQIj6Apiq0) (64K views)

This is one of Lance's core concepts and he credits it with transforming his trading career. The central idea is that **the same price does not always equal the same expected value**. Most traders fall for the fallacy that if a stock was at $50 before and is at $50 again, it's the same play. It's not — because the pattern and timing change the EV. He uses a poker analogy: a hand that has 40% chance of winning on pre-flop can climb to 80% as more cards are revealed, or vice versa. The "V" refers to a sharp selloff (the left side down) followed by a reversal (the right side up). The key insight: buying when the stock is still falling down the left side of the V (fighting the trend) has terrible EV because you're catching a falling knife. Waiting for confirmation that the move has turned — the right side — means risking less while making more, because the trend is now with you. This has huge implications for mean reversion strategies: don't fight the trend; wait for the turn and then enter.

## 3. Three Scalping Strategies

**Video:** [The 3 Scalping Trading Strategies That Made Me Millions](https://youtube.com/watch?v=2DXQqwKSwJE) (92K views)

Lance defines scalping as a surgical process: identify a key emotional level, confirm with the tape, and execute without hesitation. The three strategies he covers are:

**Strategy 1 — The Offer-Take:** An in-play stock coils tight against resistance with a large offer sitting there. Price presses up against it tighter and tighter. When a buyer takes the whole offer in one shot, you buy either the offer itself or via a stop-limit just above it, then ride the momentum of latecomers scrambling in. Exit after capturing a few cents of momentum.

**Strategy 2 — The Breakdown Scalp:** Similar concept on the short side. You wait for a large bid to get eaten, confirming selling pressure, and short into the breakdown.

**Strategy 3 — The Hidden Scalp (never talked about by other educators):** This involves anticipating the offer-take before it happens by reading order flow — identifying when a large resting order is likely to be the last line of defense before a breakout, and positioning yourself ahead of the crowd. This requires deep familiarity with Level 2 data and tape reading.

The common thread: scalping exploits micro-inefficiencies in order flow, liquidity, and short-term momentum. It demands intense focus — no distractions allowed.

## 4. Two Swing Trading Strategies

**Video:** [The 2 Swing Trading Strategies That Made Me Millions](https://youtube.com/watch?v=k-X0164r66U) (73K views)

Lance's best swing trade made him over $10M. He defines swing trading as holding positions longer than one session (days to months) using the daily chart as the primary decision timeframe. The two strategies:

**Strategy 1 — Trending Pullback:** Identify a strong trending stock on the daily chart. Wait for a pullback to a key moving average (20 EMA or 50 SMA) or a prior resistance-turned-support level. Enter when the pullback shows signs of exhausting (e.g., a doji candle, volume drying up, or a bullish reversal pattern). Target the prior highs or measured move extensions. Stop loss below the pullback low.

**Strategy 2 — Breakout Continuation:** A stock breaks out of a multi-week or multi-month consolidation range on heavy volume. Wait for the first pullback to the breakout level (which should now act as support). Enter on confirmation of support holding, then ride the next leg up. This "buy the first pullback" approach gives you a tighter stop and higher RR than chasing the initial breakout.

Key benefit: swing trading lets you capture larger directional moves without being glued to the screen all day.

## 5. Trend Trading

**Video:** [99% of Traders Don't Know How to Trade with the Trend](https://youtube.com/watch?v=ZOHG-OnQuos) (84K views)

Lance calls "the trend is your friend" the single truest quote in trading after 15 years. He discovered this painfully: when he forensically dissected his entire career's trades, every single one of his best trades was with the trend and worked immediately, while every massive loss came from fighting the trend. He defines a trend structurally — higher highs and higher lows for uptrends, lower highs and lower lows for downtrends — but emphasizes that the real edge comes from understanding how trends begin and end. An uptrend starts when buying pressure overwhelms selling at a key support level, often after a catalyst. It ends when selling pressure overwhelms buying at a resistance level. His rules: (1) Identify the dominant trend on the daily chart. (2) Only take trades in the direction of that trend. (3) Use pullbacks within the trend as entries, not breakouts against it. (4) A trend that works immediately (trade goes green fast) is confirmation you're on the right side.

## 6. Anchored VWAP Edge

**Video:** [The Anchored VWAP Edge Most Traders Never Discover](https://youtube.com/watch?v=D2P-0xh6aEM) (166K views)

Popularized by Brian Shannon (Alpha Trends), Anchored VWAP lets you set the VWAP calculation start point to a meaningful date (earnings, IPO, major breakout) rather than just the current day. Lance explains that standard VWAP shows the day's average cost basis, but anchored VWAP reveals the true average cost basis since a significant event. This helps identify: (1) Support/resistance levels where institutions accumulated shares. (2) Fair value zones where price tends to revert to. (3) Timing entries — buying when price pulls back to anchored VWAP in an uptrend. He emphasizes that VWAP works best when anchored to the most recent significant price event, and that combining it with multi-timeframe analysis (another Brian Shannon concept he credits) creates a powerful edge. He does NOT use it as an exact buy/sell signal but as a context tool to understand where value lies relative to current price.

## 7. Bollinger Band Edge

**Video:** [The Bollinger Band Edge Most Traders Never Discover](https://youtube.com/watch?v=ZZ-e9wxARSI) (76K views)

Lance says Bollinger Bands are "the main indicator I use on my intraday and daily chart" — but he explicitly does NOT use them as exact levels (never buying a break above the band). He finds them useful in three ways:

**Pendulum Concept:** Stocks without fresh news exhibit mean reversion — the further price stretches in one direction, the higher the probability and magnitude of a snapback.

**Volatility Contraction/Expansion:** When bands tighten, it signals low volatility that often precedes an explosive move (the squeeze). When bands widen, it confirms that a trend has momentum.

**Trend Context:** In a strong uptrend, price tends to ride the upper band; in a downtrend, it hugs the lower band. Bands touching or exceeding the band in a trend context is not a reversal signal — it's confirmation of trend strength. Wait for price to cross back below the middle line for trend change signals.

His prop desk saying: "Crazy can always get crazier" — don't short a stock just because it's stretched beyond the upper band.

## 8. ORB (Opening Range Breakout) Trading

**Video:** [ORB Trading Only Works If You Do These 3 Things](https://youtube.com/watch?v=9Mr2dSi-EEc) (42K views)

The Opening Range Breakout strategy trades the first 30-60 minutes of price action. Lance says ORB works, but only if you follow three specific rules:

**Rule 1 — Identify the True Opening Range:** Don't use a fixed timeframe. Let the market tell you when the opening range is established — look for the first clear high and low after the initial volatility subsides (usually after the first 15-30 minutes for most stocks).

**Rule 2 — Filter by Market Context:** ORB works best when the broader market (SPY/QQQ) is also showing clear directional bias. In a choppy, directionless market, ORB signals are unreliable regardless of the individual stock setup.

**Rule 3 — Size and Stop Matter:** Because ORB trades can have wide stops (the full range), you must size appropriately. Lance recommends risking no more than 1/3 of your daily loss limit on a single ORB trade. Enter on a confirmed break of the range with volume, not on a marginal poke-through. Place the stop at the opposite end of the range.

## 9. Momentum Trading (Ross Cameron Reaction)

**Video:** [Reacting to Ross Cameron's Momentum Trading Strategy](https://youtube.com/watch?v=sxjsqauWE9E) (181K views)

Lance analyzes Ross Cameron's small-cap momentum strategy: scan for stocks under 100M float with strong daily charts, high relative volume, and a catalyst (earnings, FDA news). Ross trades intraday continuation — buying strength during the day expecting momentum to continue. Lance's critique: the strategy itself is sound (momentum trading is a legitimate edge), but he highlights three issues: (1) Ross's claimed returns are from simulated/"paper trading" with delayed fills; real slippage on small caps is brutal. (2) The chat room format creates urgency and FOMO that leads to overtrading. (3) Small-cap shorting has asymmetric risk (halts, squeezes, dilution). His bottom line: the momentum methodology is legitimate, but beginners should be very cautious applying it with real money due to execution risks in illiquid stocks.

## 11. Trendline Trading (Tori Trades Reaction)

**Video:** [Reacting to Tori Trades Trendline Strategy](https://youtube.com/watch?v=62qSFeXa9z0) (261K views)

Tori Trades has one of the fastest-growing trading channels using a simple trendline-only approach. Lance analyzes her method: draw upward trendlines connecting higher lows, downward trendlines connecting lower highs, and trade bounces/reversals at the lines. He agrees that trendlines are a legitimate foundation of technical analysis — they represent the direction and speed of price movement. His additions: (1) Trendlines need multiple touches (3+) to be statistically meaningful. (2) The slope matters — a steep trendline breaks more often than a moderate one. (3) Combine trendlines with volume confirmation — a break of a trendline on low volume is often a fakeout. (4) Use multiple timeframe trendlines — a break of the hourly trendline is noise; a break of the daily trendline is significant. His verdict: Tori's simplicity is a strength, but traders need to understand trendline validity criteria and volume context.

## 12. Four IPO Trading Strategies

**Video:** [The 4 IPO Trading Strategies That Made Me Millions](https://youtube.com/watch?v=HAlYMtluzCk) (22.5K views)

Lance outlines four distinct IPO approaches:

**Strategy 1 — IPO Pop Trade:** Buy the opening print on high-profile IPOs with strong demand. Sell into the initial euphoria (usually within minutes to hours). Works for extreme-pop IPOs only.

**Strategy 2 — The Lockup Expiry Play:** Many IPOs dip when the lockup period expires (usually 90-180 days post-IPO) as insiders can sell. Watch for this known dip and buy when selling exhausts, anticipating the next leg up.

**Strategy 3 — Post-IPO Base Builder:** After the initial volatility settles (weeks to months), some IPOs form a base/consolidation pattern. Buy the breakout from this base for a measured move higher.

**Strategy 4 — Secondary Offering Dip:** When a post-IPO company does a secondary offering at a discount, the stock often gaps down. If the secondary is for growth (not insiders cashing out), buy the dip as institutions who couldn't get IPO shares pile in.

He emphasizes: IPOs are high-risk due to limited price history; size accordingly.

## 13. The Bobblehead Method

**Video:** [This Trading Concept Made Me One of the Best (Bobblehead Method)](https://youtube.com/watch?v=TexislSXpjs) (39K views)

The Bobblehead Method is Lance's term for how professional traders use size and conviction when the setup calls for it. The name comes from the bobblehead dolls on a trader's desk — they nod when the opportunity is right. When a stock has all the ingredients (in-play, catalyst, strong trend, volume confirmation, tight risk level), you "bobble your head" yes and size up significantly beyond your normal position. He compares this to the "Art of Betting Big" concept he discusses with Kyle Williams. The method relies on grading trades from A+ (maximum size) to D (skip entirely). Most traders make the mistake of sizing everything equally or sizing up on marginal setups. The Bobblehead Method is the framework for knowing which setups deserve 10x your standard size and which deserve zero. Warning: this only works after you've demonstrated consistent edge — beginners should size everything the same until they have enough data to grade accurately.

## 14. The Trading Playbook (Building a System)

**Video:** [What Is a Trading Playbook? (And Why YOU Need One)](https://youtube.com/watch?v=46PUq3WymmI) (47K views) | [My Day Trading Routine](https://youtube.com/watch?v=_jWWfY_pesY) (126K views)

A trading playbook is a documented system: specific setups with entry rules, exit rules, stop placement, position sizing, and market conditions. Lance says building a playbook is the single most important thing for developing traders. His own playbook includes pre-market preparation (identify in-play stocks, mark key levels), the specific setups he's looking for that day (based on market conditions), position sizing by conviction grade, and a post-market review. The playbook concept ensures you're not making decisions in the heat of the moment. He emphasizes that a playbook must be WRITTEN DOWN — not in your head. Review and refine it weekly. The goal is to remove discretion from the execution and let the playbook do the work.

## 15. Trade Sizing Strategy

**Video:** [The Trade Sizing Strategy That Made Me Millions](https://youtube.com/watch?v=eDdpTNB04ws) (44K views)

Lance uses an A-B-C-D grading system for position sizing. A+ trades get maximum size (his biggest trades of the year come from these). A trades get standard full size. B trades get half size. C trades get quarter size or skip entirely. D trades don't get traded. The key: 80% of his profits come from 5% of his trades — the A+ setups where he sizes up dramatically. He calls this "exponential bet sizing" — scaling size exponentially with the expected value of the opportunity rather than linearly. The prerequisites: you must be able to trade differently sized positions without changing your execution psychology. If doubling your normal size makes you exit early or hold too long, you're not ready for this yet. His rule: never let size change your process.

## 16. Risk Management Framework

**Video:** [15 Years of Trading Risk Management in 20 Minutes](https://youtube.com/watch?v=gb7nNveNBjg) (134K views) | [This Risk Management Strategy Saved My Career](https://youtube.com/watch?v=51l76HdSpV0) (29K views)

Lance's risk management philosophy: "Risk management is not about eliminating risk — it's about intelligently controlling exposure to maximize the odds of achieving your goals while minimizing unacceptable outcomes." His core rules:

**Daily Loss Limit:** A hard stop at the broker level. At Trillium, he started with a $150/day limit. This is non-negotiable.

**Position-Level Stop:** Stops go at levels that invalidate the thesis, not arbitrary percentages.

**Risk Per Trade:** Never risk more than 1-2% of account on a single trade (proportionally adjusted for account size).

**Correlation Risk:** Don't take multiple positions that are all correlated to the same market driver.

**The "One Bad Day" Trap:** Most blowups happen on a single bad day, not from paper cuts. The daily loss limit is your protection against the one emotional day that wipes out months of work.

**Scaling in/out:** Enter in thirds, exit in thirds. This reduces regret and improves average fills.

## 17. Fibonacci Trading (Skeptical Approach)

**Video:** [How to Use Fibonacci Levels Like a Wall Street Pro](https://youtube.com/watch?v=FHo1qxHRG98) (36K views)

Lance is openly skeptical of Fibonacci retracements, calling them "astrology for day traders." His critique: the levels get violated as often as they hold because they're self-fulfilling prophecies at best. However, he makes one exception: the 50% retracement level. He finds the 50% level to have genuine significance because it represents the midpoint of any move — a natural equilibrium point where buying and selling pressure balance. His practical framework: don't use Fib levels as exact entry/exit points. Instead, use them as rough reference zones to identify where price might find support/resistance, then confirm with price action (reversal candles, volume) and tape reading before acting. The specific level matters far less than the confluence of other factors at that zone.

## 18. Multi-Timeframe Analysis

**Video:** [How to Use Multi-Timeframe Analysis](https://youtube.com/watch?v=V-3owTiHmhw) (34K views)

Lance considers aligning multiple timeframes essential to his success, crediting Brian Shannon's book as foundational. His approach: start with the weekly chart for the macro context (is the stock in an uptrend or downtrend?). Then the daily chart for the intermediate trend and key support/resistance levels. Then the intraday chart (60-min or 15-min) for entries. The rule: all timeframes must align before taking a trade. If the weekly and daily are up but the intraday is showing a breakdown, wait for the intraday to realign. He says most traders lose money because they use a single timeframe — the 1-minute or 5-minute chart — and have no context for whether the move is with or against the broader trend. His advice: trade the daily chart direction, use the intraday for timing.

## 19. MACD Trading

**Video:** [What Traders Get Wrong About MACD](https://youtube.com/watch?v=q-jwEXbNSBs) (33K views)

Lance's take on MACD: it's a useful momentum/convergence tool but most traders misuse it by treating crossovers as buy/sell signals. He uses MACD in two specific ways: (1) **Divergence** — when price makes a higher high but MACD makes a lower high (bearish divergence) or vice versa (bullish divergence), it signals weakening momentum and potential reversal. (2) **Zero-line cross** — MACD crossing above the zero line confirms bullish momentum; crossing below confirms bearish. He explicitly warns against using MACD crossovers (when the MACD line crosses the signal line) as standalone entries — they lag too much and produce whipsaws. The divergence signal is the only MACD pattern he has real conviction in.

## 20. Options Selling (4 Golden Rules)

**Video:** [How to Sell Options Like a Wall Street Trader](https://youtube.com/watch?v=eWeGAYvjxh4) (24K views) | [How Options Trading YouTubers Fool You](https://youtube.com/watch?v=IkM7vbbSVvU) (81K views)

Selling options (naked) is Lance's favorite strategy — but he emphasizes it's extremely dangerous. His four golden rules:

**Rule 1 — Only Sell on Stocks You'd Be Fine Owning:** If you sell a put, you must be willing to own the stock at the strike. If you sell a call (covered), you must be fine selling at the strike.

**Rule 2 — Sell When IV is High, Not Low:** Most traders sell options when IV is low (complacent markets) and get crushed when volatility spikes. Sell into high IV when premium is fat.

**Rule 3 — Avoid Earnings and Binary Events:** The asymmetric risk of a gap move against you on earnings destroys the high-win-rate advantage of selling options.

**Rule 4 — Manage Size for Tail Risk:** One tail event can wipe out months of premium collection. Your sizing must account for the worst-case scenario. He compares it to writing home insurance: "Every year without a catastrophe, you get more confident — and that confidence is what kills you."

## 21. News Trading & Event-Driven

**Video:** [How to Trade the News Like a Top Wall St Trader](https://youtube.com/watch?v=eDdpTNB04ws) (31K views)

Lance's news trading framework: most news is already priced in by the time you read it. The edge comes from anticipating how the market will REACT to news, not the news itself. He looks for: (1) **Price discovery** — after earnings, the stock takes 30-60 minutes to find equilibrium. This is where the real edge lives, not in the initial gap. (2) **The fade vs. the continuation** — does the market fade the initial move (buy the dip after bad news on a strong stock) or continue in the direction of the news? (3) **Sector contagion** — news on one stock often creates opportunities in related names. He emphasizes that news trading requires the fastest execution and tightest risk management because moves reverse violently. Size should be smaller than normal.

## 22. Level 2 / Tape Reading

**Video:** [How to Read the Tape with Level 2 Data](https://youtube.com/watch?v=RKV1rncXSkg) (30K views)

Lance says tape reading was the skill that took him from being right on direction but losing money, to actually profiting. "Reading the tape" goes back to Jesse Livermore — using the size and direction of transactions to infer large buyers/sellers. Key elements: (1) **The Level 2 box (ECN book)** shows all resting bids and offers — large orders at key levels reveal where institutions are accumulating or distributing. (2) **Time and Sales (prints)** shows each executed trade — look for large prints at the bid (selling pressure) vs. the offer (buying pressure). (3) **The "iceberg" pattern** — a large order that keeps replenishing at the same price level is an iceberg; it signals institutional accumulation/distribution. (4) **The "print-through"** — when price rips through a level with massive prints, it shows real buying/selling power vs. spoofing. His rule: don't enter until you see tape confirmation that the move has genuine institutional participation.

## 23. Prediction Market Trading (5-Step Framework)

**Video:** [My Prediction Market Trading Playbook](https://youtube.com/watch?v=DtSLXf78gZo) (8.1K views)

Lance treats prediction markets (Polymarket, Kalshi) with the same rigor as stock trading. His 5-step framework: (1) **Reframe what it is** — every contract is a probability expressed as a price (65 cents = 65% chance). Your job: determine if that probability is wrong. (2) **Find mispricings** — look for markets where your knowledge gives you an edge over the crowd (election odds, regulatory decisions, sports). (3) **Bet against the crowd** — the crowd tends to overreact to recent news, creating mispricings in both directions. (4) **Manage position size by conviction** — same A-B-C-D grading as stock trading. (5) **Have an exit plan** — know when you'll take profit (75% of max value) or cut losses (20% of entry) before you enter. He warns most people lose in prediction markets because they approach them like gamblers, not traders.

## 24. Three-Step Trade Review System

**Video:** [Learn from Your Losing Trades with My 3-Step Review System](https://youtube.com/watch?v=hC4g7qY6UcQ) (19.5K views)

After every significant trade (win or loss), Lance follows a 3-step review: **Step 1 — Document Objectively:** Take screenshots (intraday + daily chart). Write down what happened, what you were feeling before/during/after, the market context, whether momentum was working, and whether this was in your playbook. **Step 2 — Identify Violations:** Were you fighting the trend? Did you follow your system and risk limits? Were there contributing factors (poor sleep, FOMO, chasing P&L, hot streak overconfidence)? **Step 3 — Create an Actionable Fix:** If you broke a rule, add a specific check to your pre-trade routine to prevent recurrence. If the trade was in your system but lost, that's acceptable — review the setup parameters for refinement. The key: reviewing winners is just as important as reviewing losers, because winners can embed bad habits that happen to pay off.

## 25. The 2026 Annual Trading Gameplan

**Video:** [Beat 99% of Traders with This 2026 Trading Gameplan](https://youtube.com/watch?v=RgztJHUBRjE) (29K views)

Lance's 7-step annual planning process that he teaches to top trading firms:

1. **Annual Review** — Analyze your full year of trades. What worked? What didn't? Calculate win rate, average win/loss, Sharpe ratio.
2. **Identify Constraints** — What's the ONE thing that if fixed would double your P&L? (Edge, execution, opportunity, or risk.)
3. **Set 3-5 Specific Goals** — Not "make more money" but measurable objectives like "trade 3 new setups" or "reduce max drawdown by 20%."
4. **Build Your Playbook** — Codify your best setups. Remove discretionary trading from your process.
5. **Fix Your Routine** — Are you getting enough sleep? Do you have a pre-market prep process? A post-market review?
6. **Find a Pod** — Surround yourself with traders who hold you accountable and review your trades.
7. **Execute and Iterate** — Review quarterly. Most people overestimate what they can do in a month but underestimate what they can do in a year.

## Key Trading Principles

### In-Play Stock Selection
Lance compares most stocks to casino games where the house has the edge — and in-play stocks to broken slot machines where the player has the edge. An in-play stock has: (1) high relative volume (2x+ its average), (2) a catalyst (news, earnings, sector move), and (3) institutional interest (visible on Level 2 / tape). Of 5,000+ tickers, only ~25 offer positive expected value on any given day, and fewer than 5 will actually move your P&L. Finding these is a professional trader's primary skill.

### The Three Reasons 95% of Traders Fail
1) **No runway** — part-time self-learners need 4-5 years minimum, not 30 days. 2) **Unrealistic expectations** — they try to monetize before competent. 3) **No structure** — no playbook, no mentor, no pod. Lance says these are fixable problems, but most traders quit before fixing them.

### Expected Value Thinking
Lance constantly emphasizes: optimize for expected value, not percentage moves. EV = (win rate × avg win) - (loss rate × avg loss) — adjusted for slippage and commissions. Beginners should trade small with positive EV setups, not chase 300% home runs.

### Trading Drawdown Protocol
Lance's process: (1) Stop IMMEDIATELY when drawdown hits threshold. (2) Review last 20 trades to find the pattern. (3) Cut size by 50%. (4) Fix the specific issue before scaling back. (5) Never try to trade your way out of a drawdown.

### Psychology from Dr. Jonathan Katz
Lance spent 5 years and $150K+ learning from Dr. Katz (a clinical psychologist to top hedge funds). Key rules: prevent tilt proactively (once angry, it's too late); emotional regulation is trainable; identify and remove triggers from your environment; separate self-worth from P&L.
