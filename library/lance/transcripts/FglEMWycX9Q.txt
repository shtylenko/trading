The next biggest segment of data, 17% of responses were issues sizing up. Part of this, of course, has a psychological component, but I wanted to dissect this topic separately. Many stated that they had issues sizing up due to being afraid of taking bigger losses. Others said they don't know how much or when to size up. Here's my general advice for this subject. A trader should only size up once they have a track record of the following. Positive expected value, meaning the trader is making P&L over a period of weeks or months. ability to consistently follow their trade rules. And finally, they consistently follow their risk management rules. With at least a few weeks of data showing you have the ability to do the above, it can be time to start sizing up. Now, here is where most people go wrong, especially in the beginning before they've gained experience handling draw downs. You want to size up far more gradually than most expect. So, when one commenter stated that he was afraid of taking bigger losses, my rebuttal would be twofold. First, risk is an inevitable part of trading. You can't ever have bigger winners without taking bigger losses over time. To grow as a trader, we must accept and internalize that risk and recognize that we will have bigger losses. But second, and more importantly, that trader was likely thinking of sizing up too aggressively at once, and that is why he was scared. We often only want to size up 10% or even 20% more at a time. Then we want to build our comfort level at that new sizing amount. The analogy I give is like that of one working out at a gym. The fastest way to grow your strength at the gym isn't by lifting as much as possible and getting injured. Instead, it is by gradually and consistently increasing weight over time while minimizing chances of injury that will set you back. Trading is no different. One simple heristic I use is to never risk an amount that if lost will damage your psychology. Most traders greatly underestimate how much their sizing will increase over the course of a year if they simply increase their risk by 10 or 20% at a time until they are comfortable with the new level. One trick I like to use to do this is what I call the sizing my desk and on it I would write how much I wanted to risk for each category of trade. Predetermining these values would minimize the amount of processing I needed to do mid-trade and also give me a clear objective way to measure whether I size the trade appropriately based on the modest growth amount I was aiming for. Over time, you gradually increase your risk amounts once the higher levels become the norm.
