Some significantly above it. Here's what each one invests in and why it has performed the way it has. Fund one, S&P 500 growth companies only. The first approach takes the S&P 500 and filters it. Instead of owning all 500 companies, you only own the ones classified as growth companies, businesses expanding their revenue and earnings at an above-average pace. The slower-growing companies, sometimes called value stocks, stable but not accelerating, are removed from the equation. Over the past decade, funds following this approach have averaged returns approximately 1 to 2% above the broader S&P 500 annually, in some years significantly more. The trade-off is that value companies tend to hold up better during market downturns. By removing them, you get more upside potential during bull markets, but slightly more exposure during corrections. For long-term investors who are comfortable with that trade-off and believe in the continued growth of leading American companies, this is a relatively straightforward way to potentially improve on the standard index return. Fund two, technology sector only. The second fund narrows focus even further. Instead of all 500 companies, it only invests in the technology companies within the S&P 500, roughly 65 to 70 companies out of the 500. Technology has been the dominant growth engine of the global economy over the past decade. Cloud computing, software as a service, artificial intelligence infrastructure, digital advertising, these sectors grew significantly faster than the broader economy, and the companies leading them grew significantly faster than the average S&P 500 company. The risk here is concentration. When you reduce 500 companies down to 65, you amplify both the gains and the losses. Technology corrections can be sharp and fast, but for long-term investors with a long time horizon who believe technology remains central to economic growth, this concentration has historically been rewarded. The fourth approach is based on a concept called momentum, the observed tendency of assets that have been rising in price to continue rising for a period of time before reversing. Momentum investing funds track the S&P 500 companies showing the strongest price momentum, typically the top 100 companies ranked by how quickly their stock price has been rising, and rotate holdings as momentum shifts. This is more active than traditional index investing. The fund buys and sells positions more frequently to stay aligned with current momentum. That means slightly more activity and somewhat more complexity than simply holding all 500 companies. Over the past decade, funds using this approach averaged approximately 18% annually. The academic research behind momentum as a return factor dates back several decades and has been documented across multiple markets and time periods. The risk is that momentum can reverse quickly. Here is the part most finance videos skip. It does not matter which funds you choose if your investing works against you. The single most destructive pattern in investing is buying when markets are rising and selling when markets fall. It feels rational in the moment. When prices are going up, optimism is high and buying feels safe. When prices are falling, fear takes over and selling feels like protection. But this behavior, buying high and selling low, is the opposite of what builds wealth. It locks in losses and eliminates the recovery gains that follow every historical market decline. The strategy that works is simpler and harder than most people expect. Always be buying. Set up an automatic investment that leaves your account on a fixed schedule, weekly, bi-weekly, monthly, and goes directly into the funds you've chosen. That transfer happens regardless of market conditions. When markets are up, you buy. When markets are down, you buy. And when markets drop significantly, that's when you buy more aggressively if you have the cash reserves to do it. Every major market decline in history has eventually been followed by a full recovery and new highs. The 2008 financial crisis, the 2020 pandemic crash, the 2022 correction, every one of them was followed by a recovery that rewarded the investors who stayed in and penalized the ones who sold. Recessions and market crashes are not anomalies. They're features of the economic system. Understanding that they're cycles, not endings, is what separates investors who build wealth from investors who destroy it by reacting emotionally to short-term events. The funds on this list can potentially give you access to above-average long-term returns, but only if the strategy holding them in place is strong enough to survive the inevitable periods of decline.
