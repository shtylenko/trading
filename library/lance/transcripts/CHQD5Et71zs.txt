There's also a third type of scalp that doesn't often get addressed, and that is making the spread, especially in a liquid name. Most traders think of scalping as hitting bids and lifting offers to capture short bursts of momentum. Spread making is different. Instead of aggressively taking liquidity, you provide the liquidity. You're placing resting limit orders and spreading [snorts] names ahead of large bids or offers with the aim to capture the difference between the spread as other participants are crossing and hitting the bid and paying the offer. The edge here does not come from predicting direction. It comes from understanding market microstructure. When a stock is in relative equilibrium, but thin, market participants who need urgency will cross the spread. They'll pay that offer up there and hit the bid down there. In this sense, by providing that liquidity, you are acting more like a market maker than a momentum trader. To do this well, you must really understand market microstructure of order types, queue priority, and hidden versus visible orders. My trainer was incredible at this, and one of the most impressive trades I've ever seen someone make was when he made hundreds of thousands of dollars in minutes during the Facebook IPO. When there was over a dollar spread and just absolutely incredible amounts of volume going off on both sides of the market, he took such quick and aggressive advantage of that, immediately just slamming in huge bids only to just as quickly kick his offers out and his size out a point or two higher, and repeating it again and again before that spread compressed and the stock drifted lower.
