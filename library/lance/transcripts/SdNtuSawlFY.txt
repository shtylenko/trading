Title: Avoid These 3 Trading Traps #trading #fomo #tradingpsychology
URL: https://youtube.com/watch?v=SdNtuSawlFY
======================================================================

One of the most common psychological traps is overtrading because you need to be right. You become emotionally attached to a bias or thesis. At that point, it's no longer about making money. It becomes personal. The stock disagreed with your ego, and now you're fighting the chart like it insulted your career choice at Thanksgiving dinner. How many of you have fallen victim to revenge trading? I've done it, and I'm sure you've done it, too. You take a stop out only to watch the stock immediately move in the original direction you expected. That feeling is brutal. It feels like the market personally targeted your stop before rocketing higher without you. So, what do we do? We instantly jump back in emotionally, usually with worse entries, worse sizing, and worse judgment. Now, instead of one controlled loss, we have created multiple. And then there's a king of all overtrading emotions, FOMO. Fear of missing out has probably destroyed more accounts than bad technical analysis ever has. You see everyone on Twitter posting giant gains. Your friends are all short some stock that's collapsing. The move looks unstoppable. You feel physically sick sitting there while everyone else seems printing money. So, you chase. Even though the setup wasn't in your playbook, even though the risk-reward now sucks. But emotions don't care about logic when FOMO takes over. As one trading psychologist I know has so astutely commented, traders engage in FOMO trading because to them, the pain of missing out on a move exceeds the pain of taking a loss. Another psychological cause of overtrading is having a biased view of what the stock should be doing. Maybe you read an analyst report or own the stock as an investment. Either way, external views of what the stock should do has clouded your judgment over what the price action is doing.
