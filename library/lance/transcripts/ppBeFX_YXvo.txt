I ran a trading floor for one of the top proprietary trading firms in the world. And here are the top three traits I looked for when hiring a trading candidate. I'm Lance Brightstein, one of Wall Street's top traders with over $und00 million in verified profits. Not only was I Trillium's top trader, I also built and ran their Chicago office for many years. Every fall, we would interview dozens of qualified candidates from every impressive background you could imagine. students from top universities, division 1 athletes, competitive gamers, and more. The first trait I looked for was that the candidate had what I call a powerful why. A powerful why is their reason for why they want to become a trader. Trading is an incredibly hard job with a steep multi-year learning curve during which you're frustrated and making minimal money. If you don't have a powerful why, when you find yourself staring into the abyss and that will inevitably occur as a trader, in those key moments of doubt, you're going to convince yourself to go get that easy accounting gig or just to become an influencer or maybe even better become a trust fund baby. I know this because I was there too. It took me almost 2 years to become profitable and I was so close to leaving the job. The only thing that kept me with it was my powerful why. Many get into trading for the money and actually there's nothing wrong with that. The desire to be financially free can be extremely powerful. The desire to provide for your family. This was certainly a huge factor for me. Other people have a true love for markets. They simply love trading. They love the game. They, like myself, can't imagine a job more fun than trying to beat the market. Others are just insanely competitive. It absolutely kills them not to succeed at trading. Failure isn't an option. They thrive on the struggle, pushing themselves to get better each day. The truth is, we don't have just one. It's a mix of reasons, but I wanted to make sure that the candidates's reasons, whatever they were, were exceptionally strong. Could they relay a cohesive story as why they had that hunger to give it at least 2 years at the job? That was key at Trillium, finding traders who would stick it out at least that time period, because anything shorter isn't giving it a proper chance. If you're thinking, "My Y is probably strong enough and I'm probably tough enough." Are you sure? Test your why by watching my video on why trading is a terrible career and see if you still want to pursue it. It's linked in the description. The second trait I looked for is what I like to call coachability. I define coachability as being able to constructively receive feedback without getting defensive and then most importantly being able to incorporate that feedback as quickly as possible. A huge part of being coachable also means having no ego, which is always important for success in trading. If you think you're too smart or always right, you don't listen to your trainer and you don't listen to the market either. Both put you on the fast track out the door. There's a reason why trading firms love college athletes. Not only are they extremely competitive, but highle athletes have spent their whole lives receiving tough feedback from coaches and persevering through losses. They've learned to emotionally accept a loss and to learn from it the first time. One of my favorite trainees was a college football player. He wasn't some MIT brainiac, but it didn't matter. He was exceptionally good at taking instruction from me, saying, "Yes, coach." And incorporating it. The speed in which we can incorporate feedback is actually what I find to be the number one determinant of how quickly a trader progresses. Essentially, a powerful Y is how long of a runway a trader will give the job. Then coachability speaks to how fast they will progress while on that runway. If you use a daily report card, the concept I talk about in this video, you can use them to better track and incorporate feedback on your own so that you grow at a faster rate. The third and final of my top three traits is a team first mindset. This is one of the key differences in a trading floor versus being a retail trader at home. Operating on a trading floor, you can control the environment and build a culture. I was a strong believer that if traders work together, the whole will always be greater than the parts. The Trillium Chicago office operated like a Navy Seal team thanks to the culture I helped construct. We held each other to a high standard. We shared intel and split up work responsibilities. And most of all, we supported each other during the lows while celebrating with each other during the highs. Trading is too hard of a job to go at alone. That's why I have long stressed that traders at home do what they can to link up and learn with other traders. Many traders now use Discord and other tools to replicate the environment of an office. In fact, this team first mindset and working in a team-based culture is one of the biggest lessons I learned in my 15 years of trading, which I share in this video with the top lessons that brought me massive success.
