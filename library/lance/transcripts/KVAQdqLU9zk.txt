There are four main strategies I focus on. The first is the opening drive. This is one of the most powerful patterns in IPO trading because the market is still discovering the correct price. When a new listing opens for trading, there's often a massive imbalance between buyers and sellers. If demand overwhelms supply, the stock can trend aggressively in one direction for the first 10 to 30 minutes. What I'm watching for here is strong relative volume, aggressive bids stepping up, and price holding above the opening range. If the stock opens and immediately starts grinding higher with shallow pullbacks, that's often the opening drive. These moves can be incredibly powerful because there is no overhead resistance. The stock has never traded before, so there are no trapped longs waiting to sell. The second strategy is the counter drive. Not every IPO goes straight up and never pulls back. In fact, some of the best trades happen after that initial opening drive exhausts itself. If a stock opens extremely hot and rips vertically, eventually buyers get tired. Early longs start taking profits, momentum traders exit, and the stock begins to fade. That first real pullback can create an opportunity for a counter trend trade. This requires experience because you're stepping against the dominant momentum. I'm looking for exhaustion signals, failed pushes to new highs, heavy selling into strength, or a break of intraday support, or break of prior bar lows. When that happens, the unwind can be fast because momentum traders all rush for the exit at the same time. The third strategy I employ is the later day breakout. Many IPOs will have that strong opening drive and then consolidate for hours. During that consolidation, the market is essentially deciding where the next leg should go. If the stock builds a tight base and then breaks above that range with strong volume, that breakout can lead to another powerful leg higher. Again, the key advantage with IPOs is the lack of historical resistance. When we break to new highs in a stock that has only traded for a few hours, there aren't many natural sellers left, and there's so many people that are often scrambling to buy. That's why breakouts in strong IPOs can run much further than people expect. The fourth trading strategy is overnight momentum. This one is simple but incredibly effective during hot markets. If an IPO closes near the highs of the day with strong momentum and heavy volume, and especially if it did strategy three, that midday breakout, there's a good chance that excitement and that urgency to build into a position carries into the next morning. News spreads, social media picks it up, and retail traders get even more hyped about the ticker overnight. And suddenly, you have a fresh wave of new demand at the next open. This strategy works best when the broader market is bullish and the IPO is already a popular name.
