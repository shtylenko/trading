What is reading the tape? The term reading the tape goes back to the days of Jesse Livermore from the famous book Reminiscences of a Stock Operator. Back in the day, there was a ticker tape that would print out the time and sales information for every transaction that would occur in each stock. Traders would use this information to know the price of the stock and create stock charts with it. The phrase reading the tape refers to traders using the size and direction of transactions to infer knowledge about large buyers and sellers and bet on which price the stock would go next as a result. Of course, much has changed since then with the advent of electronic trading. Nowadays, reading the tape means using what's called the level two box and the ECN book along with the time and sales window, also known as the prints window. You use these to discern useful information about the price action of the stock. So, what are all these things? Allow me to explain. A level two window is a real-time display of the current buy and sell orders sitting in the market at different price levels. It shows which market makers or ECNs, electronic communication networks like Nasdaq or Arca, are bidding or offering shares, the prices they are quoting, and the size available at each price level. Traders use it to gauge short-term supply, demand, liquidity, and potential support or resistance areas. Always on a level two box, you will have the bids on the left side with the highest bid at the top. Below the top bid, you will have less competitive bids displayed in descending order below. And on the right side, you will have the offers or the ask with the lowest price at the top. Below the top offer, you will have less competitive offers displayed in ascending order below. The top line of both the bid and the offer make up what is referred to as a national best bid and offer or NBBO. Those prices represent the best price at which you could buy or sell the instrument. Keep in mind, this level two box only displays visible liquidity.
