Why do IPOs tend to have such a big pop on day one? So, there's a few structural reasons, and this trend is very much real and backed by data. First, investment banks intentionally price IPOs slightly below what they believe the market will pay. This practice is known as IPO underpricing. Banks do this because their job isn't just to sell shares. It's to make sure the deal is successful. A strong first-day performance makes institutional clients happy. It makes the company look good and helps the bank secure future deals. In other words, they would rather leave a little money on the table than risk the IPO failing. Second, supply is extremely constrained on day one. Only a small portion of the company's total shares are actually available for trading. Meanwhile, demand can be massive from institutions, hedge funds, and retail traders who were not able to get an allocation. That imbalance between limited supply and overwhelming demand is what creates the initial surge. And like I said, the data backs this up. Historically, IPOs have produced significant average first-day gains. Over the past few decades in the US, the average first-day return has been roughly 15% to 20% in hotter markets. It can be much, much higher. During the 2020 to 2021 bull market, following the COVID panic, the average IPO pop was close to 30% to 40% on the first day due to the prevalence of market euphoria. Of course, those are averages. Some IPOs explode much more than that, while others barely move at all. Another huge factor that determines how explosive an IPO will be is the float. Float is the number of shares that are actually available for trading. In many IPOs, the float is tiny relative to demand. The company might be worth billions of dollars on paper, but only a small portion of shares are actually released to the public at first. That tight supply creates an environment where price can move extremely fast.
