So, let's take a look at what no man's land tends to look like. First, traders most often get caught up in no man's land after a stock has made a leg in one direction, has had the counter leg, but then is chopping around while volatility contracts. Another common place is after a stock has broken out and made a leg, then is consolidating ahead of the next leg. So, many traders wrongfully try to position themselves ahead of the real breakout and paper cut themselves up in the aggregate.
