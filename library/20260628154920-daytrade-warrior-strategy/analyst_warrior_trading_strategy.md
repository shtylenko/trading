## 1. Core Trading Philosophy

### The Fundamental Belief
Ross Cameron's entire strategy rests on one core conviction: **momentum day trading on low-float, catalyst-driven stocks during the morning hours** is the most reliable way to generate outsized returns. He does not trade forex, crypto, options (as a primary strategy), or long-term investments. He trades US equities exclusively.

> *"I wouldn't say that you can only trade in the morning or you can only trade in the afternoon. However, I've always found that the first 2 hours are the best."*

### Why Low Float Matters
The central insight: stocks with a low number of shares available to trade (the float) can move dramatically on relatively small buying pressure. A catalyst (news, earnings, FDA approval, acquisition) triggers demand, and when there's limited supply (low float), price explodes.

- **Hot market:** Float of less than 20 million shares
- **Cold market:** Float of less than 5 million shares (tighter conditions require even less supply)
- **Target move potential:** Stocks should have potential to move at least 10-20%, with targets of 50-75-100%+

> *"If a stock is only going up 4 or 5%, a small piece of 4 or 5%, even if you're using leverage, even if you're trading with options, isn't significant. So I think you want to look for stocks that have the potential to go up at least 10 to 20%, but really with a target of 50, 75, 100% or more."*

### The Edge Thesis
Cameron's edge comes from:
1. **Speed of entry** — Buying the first 5-minute candle that makes a new high during a momentum move
2. **Tight risk control** — Small average losses (~$1,300 on his main account, ~$30-50 on small account)
3. **Scaling into winners** — Adding to positions that are working, never averaging down
4. **Market selection** — Only trading stocks with catalyst + low float + high relative volume

### Type of Trader
- **Pure day trader** — Almost never holds overnight (exceptions: very rare)
- **Not a swing trader** — Positions held minutes to hours, rarely more than a day
- **Not a scalper in the quick-scalp sense** — His trades typically last 5-60 minutes, not seconds
- **Cash account trader** — Avoids PDT rule limitations by using cash accounts for small challenges

## 3. The ACD Pattern (Core Entry)

This is THE pattern Ross Cameron teaches as his primary entry. It is a **momentum continuation** pattern, NOT a reversal pattern.

### The Pattern Structure

```
Phase A (Accumulation / Consolidation)
  ↕ The stock trades sideways in a tight range after an initial move up
  ↕ Volume is typically lower during this phase
  ↕ Supply and demand are in balance

Phase C (Crack / Breakout)
  ↖ The first 5-minute candle breaks above the consolidation range
  ↖ This is the entry trigger
  ↖ Must be accompanied by increasing volume

Phase D (Distribution / Continuation)
  ↗ The stock continues higher as momentum traders enter
  ↗ Cameron adds to position through this phase
  ↗ He starts taking profits in pieces as resistance levels are hit
```

### Contradiction: Is it really ACD or Micro Pullback?

In practice across the 98 transcripts, Cameron uses TWO slightly different descriptions for what appears to be the same pattern:

**Version 1 (ACD / Flat Top Breakout):**
- Stock moves up, then goes sideways (consolidation)
- You buy on the first 5-minute candle that makes a NEW HIGH above the consolidation
- "Buy when the first candle makes a new high — boom, that's the spot"

**Version 2 (Micro Pullback):**
- Stock moves up, THEN pulls back slightly (not all the way to the bottom)
- You buy the pullback into support (often VWAP or EMA)
- "I like looking for stocks that are experiencing a micro pullback"

**Reconciliation:** Cameron uses both depending on market conditions. In strong momentum (high relative volume > 10x), he prefers the flat-top breakout. In choppier conditions, he buys micro pullbacks to support. Both are patterns within an established uptrend, never counter-trend.

### Entry Execution Steps

1. **Identify the setup on the 5-minute chart** — Stock has moved up on catalyst, now consolidating
2. **Set an alert at consolidation high** — "First candle to make a new high"
3. **Watch for the breakout candle** — 5-minute candle that closes above consolidation
4. **Enter immediately** — Buy at market using hotkey (Shift+1 for him)
5. **Add on confirmation** — If the candle continues higher, add more shares
6. **Set stop** — Below the consolidation range or below VWAP

### Time-Based Entry Rule
- **Best time for entries:** 7:00 AM - 10:00 AM ET
- **Volume ramp:** "In 2 minutes we're going to see more volume increase" (referring to 7:00 AM)
- **Stop trading:** "I should stop trading after 12:00 PM"
- **The "9:00 AM surge":** Volume typically spikes again when regular market hours begin

### Multiple Contradictions on Time

| Statement Source | What He Said |
|-----------------|--------------|
| Beginner training | "I should stop trading after 12:00 PM" |
| Advanced training | "I wouldn't say you can only trade in the morning or only in the afternoon. I've always found the first 2 hours are the best" |
| Live streams | Continues trading through 11:00 AM-12:00 PM regularly |
| Small account | Trades one entry/exit, often done by 9:30-10:00 AM |

**Resolution:** The core rule is "trade the first 2-3 hours." He extends this on good days and cuts it short on bad days. The "stop at 12" is a rule for beginners to prevent overtrading.

## 4. Additional Entry Patterns

### 4.1 VWAP Bounce / Dip Buy
One of his most frequently used entries:
- **Setup:** Stock is in an uptrend, pulls back to VWAP (Volume Weighted Average Price)
- **Entry:** Buy the dip when price touches VWAP and starts curling up
- **Stop:** Below VWAP by a few cents
- **Target:** Retest of the day's high

> *"I get better results when I take a dip off the EMA or VWAP, and then just hold as it goes back up."*

### 4.2 Opening Range Breakout (ORB)
- **Setup:** First 5-minute candle of the regular session (9:30 AM) establishes the opening range
- **Entry:** Buy when price breaks above the high of the opening range candle
- **This is the same ACD pattern**, just applied to the 9:30 opening range specifically
- Used less frequently than his "gap and go" entries

### 4.3 Breaking News Entry
- **Trigger:** Out of nowhere, a news event drives volume spike
- **Action:** Buy immediately — "I bought right into the squeeze"
- **Risk:** Higher risk because there's no consolidation to reference
- **Mitigation:** Smaller position size until the pattern forms

### 4.4 Short Squeeze Entry
- **Trigger:** Rapid price increase driven by short sellers covering
- **Entry:** Buy when the squeeze accelerates over a key resistance level
- **Exit:** Take profits more aggressively — squeeze moves can reverse violently
- **Note:** He does NOT short stocks. He only buys long-side entries, even on squeeze candidates.

> *"I'm not short selling... I only go long."*

### 4.5 Pullback to Support (EMA Bounce)
- Uses the 9-period EMA or 20-period EMA on the 1-minute chart
- Buys when price touches the EMA during an uptrend and bounces
- Tight stop below the EMA

## 5. Exit Strategy

### The 1:1 Profit/Loss Ratio (Core Finding)

> *"My average winners are about $1,300, average losers are about $1,300-$1,400. It's about a one-to-one ratio. But you can be profitable with a one-to-one ratio as long as your accuracy is higher than 50%."*

**This is the single most important exit statistic.** Cameron explicitly states his average winner and average loser are approximately equal. His edge does NOT come from a favorable reward-to-risk ratio. His edge comes from:
1. **Win rate > 50%** (he estimates ~55-65% accuracy)
2. **Position management** (scaling into winners increases average win size)
3. **Quick loss cutting** (small losers don't become big losers)

### The 1:1 Ratio is PLANNED, Not Accidental
He designs trades to have equal risk and reward:
- Risk $0.10/share with position sized such that max loss = X dollars
- Target $0.10/share profit = same X dollars
- If the pattern works, you get your 10 cents. If not, you lose 10 cents.

### Exit Methods (in order of frequency)

**1. Immediate stop loss (most common):**
- "30 cent max loss rule" — on his standard trades, if a stock is down more than 30 cents, he bails
- On small account: ~10-20 cents per share max loss
- "If it's down more than 30 cents, I gotta bail"

**2. Scaling out into strength:**
- Takes 1/3 to 1/2 off at first resistance
- Moves stop to breakeven on remaining
- Takes another 1/3 at next resistance
- Lets the last portion run with a trailing stop

**3. Profit target at known resistance:**
- "Target at $20 and 50 cents" — specific price levels from pre-market analysis
- Resistance levels identified on the daily chart and pre-market levels

**4. Trailing stop:**
- Once the stock is up significantly, he uses a trailing stop
- "Once you're up 50 cents on a stock that cost $3, you can trail your stop"
- He'll trail by 10-20 cents depending on volatility

**5. Time stop:**
- If a stock hasn't moved within 30-60 minutes, he exits
- "If it's just sitting there, I'm out. I want immediate resolution."

### The "Breakout or Bailout" Mentality
> *"If I get into a trade, it's because I'm looking at a very specific pattern and I'm expecting almost immediate resolution."*

If the stock does NOT make a strong move within 5-15 minutes of entry, he exits. The pattern MUST resolve quickly or he's wrong.

## 6. Position Sizing and Risk Management

### Small Account Position Sizing ($2,000)
The formula is simple:
- **Target gain per day:** 10% of account = $200
- **Stop loss per day:** 10% of account = $200 max
- **Per-trade risk:** $30-50 max loss
- **Share size calculation:** Max loss ($30) ÷ Max loss per share ($0.10-0.30) = 100-300 shares

### Main Account Position Sizing (Large)
- **Per-trade risk:** $1,300-1,400 (average loss)

- **Max position:** Limited by broker (he calls broker to set max position size limits)

He frequently emphasizes the importance of knowing when to stop. The $1,000 target is a psychological anchor — once he's up that much, he's satisfied and less likely to give it back.

- **Rule:** Stop trading after a max loss on the day (amount depends on account size)

## 7. Scaling (Pyramiding)

### What Scaling Is (in Cameron's Framework)
Scaling means adding to a position as it moves in your favor, and taking partial profits as it continues higher. This is NOT averaging down.

**Scaling vs. No Scaling:**

| Strategy | Entry | Exit | Result |
|----------|-------|------|--------|
| No scaling | 1,000 shares at $9.50 | 1,000 shares at $10.00 | Profit: $500 |
| Scaling | 300 at $9.50, 300 at $9.60, 400 at $9.70 | Sell 1/3 at each target | Larger profit, lower average risk |

### When to Scale
- **Only into strength:** Never add to a losing position
- **On the ACD pattern:** Add on the first 5-minute new high, add again on the second
- **On VWAP bounce:** Add on each successive touch of VWAP that holds
- **After the surge:** Take partial profits at resistance levels, let rest run

### When NOT to Scale
> *"When to avoid scaling: in a choppy market, when you don't have conviction, when the stock isn't moving cleanly."*

- **Choppy sideways markets** — scaling adds risk in a directionless market
- **Low conviction setups** — if you're unsure, one entry one exit only
- **End of day** — less time for the pattern to resolve
- **After a stop loss hit** — never try to "make it back" by scaling in

### The Intermediate Scaling Strategy
For beginner/intermediate traders, Cameron recommends:
- **1 entry, partial exit, trail the rest**
- Start with full position
- Take 1/2 off at first target
- Move stop to breakeven
- Let the rest run with trailing stop

### Contradictions on Scaling Frequency
- In the scaling master class, he advocates multi-entry/multi-exit
- In many daily recaps, he simply does "one entry, one exit"
- **Resolution:** The daily morning show/live stream often has more complex scaling. The recaps often simplify to one entry/exit for educational clarity.

## 11. Trading Psychology and Rules

### The Trader's Mindset

**Rule 1: Discipline over prediction**
> *"The second leading cause of failure comes from traders who have learned a strategy, but they lack the discipline to follow the rules of the strategy."*

**Rule 2: Never catch the whole move**
> *"One of the things that I found in my career is that you're never going to capture the entire move. If a stock goes up 50%, you're never going to capture the full 50%."*

**Rule 3: Losses are part of the business**
> *"You better get really good at losing. Get really good at identifying your stop loss, your bailout criteria, and when you see it, you get out and you get out early before the loss gets bigger."*

**Rule 4: No revenge trading**
> *"Today I was a risk manager. I took a little bit of risk, but not a lot. I trade with smaller position size. I made a little bit of money. I'm finishing the day in the green. That's solid."*

**Rule 5: The one trade that makes the day**
> *"You know at a certain point say, 'You know what, I need a thousand dollars.' That is enough for one day. That's a good day. Take it, shut it down, go do something else."*

### The "Loser Cognitive Loop" He Warns About

Cameron identifies a destructive cycle:
1. Take a loss
2. Feel the need to "make it back" immediately
3. Take a lower-quality setup
4. Chase the trade (buy above your planned entry)
5. Take a bigger loss
6. Repeat until blowing up

**His solution:** Strict daily loss limit, strict per-trade loss limit, stop trading immediately after hitting either.

### Emotional States He Tracks
- **Scared money:** Trading while worried about losses → leads to hesitation, missed entries
- **Overconfident money:** After 3 green days → leads to oversized positions, reckless entries
- **Neutral / focused:** The ideal state — "just executing the plan"

### The "Green Day Shut It Down" Rule
A consistent pattern across his trading: once he's green for the day, he dramatically reduces risk. He may take one more small trade, but he's protecting the green.

### The "Red Day" Recovery
On a red day:
- Stop trading after max loss
- Review the trades objectively (worksheet)
- Identify: was it strategy failure or execution failure?
- If strategy failure: adjust filters
- If execution failure: recommit to plan

## 12. Market Condition Analysis

### Market Regimes He Identifies

| Regime | Characteristics | His Response |
|--------|----------------|-------------|
| Strong Bull | Broad market up, many gappers > 10% | Full size, aggressive scaling |
| Choppy / Sideways | Gappers fade quickly, patterns don't resolve | Reduce size, one entry/exit only |
| Bear / Gap Down | Market opens down, few gappers above 5% | Very small size or sit out |

When the market is slow:
- **Tighter float filter:** Under 5M shares (instead of 20M)
- **Lower price:** Focus on $2-$4 range
- **Smaller expectations:** Reduce profit targets
- **Wait for catalyst:** Only trade if there's a clear news trigger

> *"In a colder market, I tend to focus on stocks with a float of less than 5 million shares because during a colder market, things don't always get going."*

### The "Christmas Tree" / No-Setup Day
Some days there are simply no good setups. His rule:
- If the gap scanner returns < 5 good candidates, don't force trades
- If the top gapper has a float > 50M, skip it
- If the top gapper is under $1, skip it
- Better to do nothing than take a bad trade

### The PDT Rule Impact and FINRA Changes
Cameron discusses the recently approved FINRA amendment to the Pattern Day Trader rule:
- Old rule: $25,000 minimum for pattern day traders in margin accounts
- **Cash accounts:** Are NOT subject to PDT — this is his workaround

## 16. Breakout vs. Bailout Philosophy

This is the CENTRAL organizing principle of Cameron's strategy.

### The Philosophy
> *"If I get into a trade, it's because I'm looking at a very specific pattern and I'm expecting almost immediate resolution."*

**You break out (the pattern resolves upward) or you bail out (exit at a small loss).**

There is no "wait and see." There is no "hold through the pullback." There is:
- **If the candle closes making a new high:** Hold, add, profit
- **If the candle does NOT make a new high within 1-2 bars:** BAIL

### Why This Works
1. **Small losses are easy to recover from** — bailing at $0.10/shr loss vs. holding for a $1.00/shr loss
2. **Pattern must confirm itself quickly** — if the setup is correct, it should work NOW
3. **Mental energy** — no agonizing over "should I stay or should I go"

### What "Bailout" Does NOT Mean
- It does NOT mean taking a tiny loss on every trade (he holds winners for big moves)
- It does NOT mean exiting at the first sign of a dip (he allows pullbacks within an uptrend)
- It does NOT mean switching to a different strategy (always momentum, always gappers)

### The Bailout Decision Tree
```
Enter on 5-min candle new high
  ↓
Candle closes green AND above the breakout level?
  ├── YES → Hold, look for add-on-next candle
  └── NO → Exit immediately (bailout)
       ↓
Next candle:
  ├── Makes new high? → Add size
  └── Pulls back to VWAP? → Hold, if VWAP holds
      └── Breaks VWAP? → Exit (bailout)
```
