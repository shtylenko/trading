Not everything is all roses though trading at a proprietary trading firm. There are some cons. Con number one is external pressure. When someone is paying you a salary and providing you with capital, you ultimately need to make sure you meet their expectations or else bye-bye job. The great part is most firms tend to give you a pretty long leash given the long learning curve. But nevertheless, you don't have the ability to pursue the job on your own timeline. Con number two is of course the pay split. At any trading firm, they'll be keeping a piece of your profits. How much depends on how the firm is structured, whether you are paid a salary, and whether you need to put down first loss capital as a deposit. But unlike trading on your own, you can be sure you will not be taking home 100% of your profits. I've had so many retail traders tell me that they never be willing to do a pay split. That seems foolish to me. Would you rather keep 100% of X or would you rather keep a majority% of 3X? Oftentimes, the gap between the retail environment and the pro environment really is that big. Con number three, most firms tend to specialize in a certain style of trading. It isn't their way or the highway by any means, but due to the environment and dedication of firm resources, most people do tend to burrow into the same niche that the firm has expertise in. If the firm specializes in lightning fast speed and technology, it wouldn't make sense to focus on long-term fundamental value investing there. Similarly, if the firm is highly quantitative and provides tons of resources for programmers and statisticians, it wouldn't make sense to be there solely to perform discretionary trading based on technicals. Con number four, less freedom. This one is obvious. You can't show up to the office and bring your pets with you. While the dress policy, especially for established traders, is pretty relaxed, you definitely can't dress up to work on Halloween as a male stripper. And yes, I did 100% actually try that once.
