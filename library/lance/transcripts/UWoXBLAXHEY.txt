Step number one, reframe what a prediction market actually is. A prediction market is a very straightforward bet based on an outcome of an event. The way these markets work is you'll have a prompt, and this could be something like who will win an election or will it rain today or will a word get said at a press conference. And then you can buy the outcomes that are yes or no, and these outcomes are sold as contracts. Essentially, these are what traders call binary markets. Every contract is just a probability expressed as a price. If a contract trades at 65 cents, the market is saying there's a 65% chance this event happens. Your only job is to answer one question, is that probability wrong? And buy enough to overcome any fees, frictions, and the risk. That's it. If you don't have a strong opinion that the probability itself is mispriced, you have no business being involved. Just like with stocks, step two is that edge is non-negotiable. Information asymmetry, this is where some participants have information or insights others don't, and this even includes insider information. There have been dozens of examples of inside information being acted upon in the prediction markets, like the person who made $500,000 ahead of Maduro being captured out of Venezuela. Number two, speed of reaction to new information. The same way algorithms trade inflation data in the stock market, there are now algos reacting to news releases on prediction markets. Number three, structural or behavioral inefficiencies. Biases and beliefs influence how people bet. There are times where you can arbitrage a prediction market's pricing of an event versus markets for stocks, futures, or crypto.
