If there were, fixing it would be way easier. The hard part is you need to identify the reason specific to you and your situation. One trader does it because they're bored, another because they need to feel right, another because they're addicted to the dopamine hit. Same behavior, different root causes. Don't worry though, I'm going to explain this better and give you the solutions. First, how do you know that you're actually overtrading? A lot of beginner traders make the very hindsight mistake of thinking that all the trades that worked were the ones they should have taken, and all their losses were automatically overtrades. Obviously, this is not how trading works. I define overtrading as the inability to stick to our predefined trading playbook. That definition actually brings us to the number one cause of overtrading. The biggest reason why most traders overtrade is they don't actually have a playbook. You trade on vibes because you don't have a clearly defined framework that objectively tells you what trades qualify and what trades do not. This is where things get extra frustrating because you know the rules and you find yourself breaking them anyways. One of the most common psychological traps is overtrading because you need to be right. You become emotionally attached to a bias or thesis. At that point, it's no longer about making money. It becomes personal. The stock disagreed with your ego and now you're fighting the chart like it insulted your career choice at Thanksgiving dinner. How many of you have fallen victim to revenge trading? I've done it and I'm sure you've done it, too. You take a stop out only to watch the stock immediately move in the original direction you expected. That feeling is brutal. It feels like the market it personally targeted your stop before rocketing higher without you. So, what do we do? We instantly jump back in emotionally. Usually with worse entries, worse sizing, and worse judgment. Now, instead of one controlled loss, we have created multiple. And then there's the king of all overtrading emotions, FOMO. Fear of missing out has probably destroyed more accounts than bad technical analysis ever has. You see everyone on Twitter posting giant gains. Your friends are all short some stock that's collapsing. The move looks unstoppable. You feel physically sick sitting there while everyone else seems to be printing money. So, you chase. Even though the setup wasn't in your playbook, even though the risk-reward now sucks. But emotions don't care about logic when FOMO takes over. As one trading psychologist I know has so astutely commented, traders engage in FOMO trading because to them, the pain of missing out on the move exceeds the pain of taking a loss. Another psychological cause of overtrading is having a biased view of what the stock should be doing. Maybe you read an analyst report or own the stock as an investment. Either way, external views of what the stock should do has clouded your judgment over what the price action is doing. At Trillium, it was so common for us to make the mistake of letting our fundamental views take over, and it's one of the worst mistakes you can make. Always, always, always trade charts and setups rather than views. So, how do we actually overcome all these psychological causes? The first step behind any behavioral change is always awareness. We need to build the skill set of identifying our feelings in the moment and recognizing our emotional state when we are taking the trade, even if in the moment we can't initially stop ourselves. In psychology, you cannot interrupt a pattern you do not consciously recognize. The moment you begin to identify your emotional state in real time, whether it's revenge, boredom, FOMO, frustration, greed, you create separation between the impulse and the action. Even if you still take the bad trade initially, building the habit of recognizing the emotion is massively important because awareness weakens that automatic behavior. And ironically, the goal at first is not perfection. The goal is simply shortening the delay between the mistake and recognizing it. Maybe today you realize you over traded an hour later. Eventually, it becomes 10 minutes later, then during the trade, then before the trade. That progression is how real behavioral change actually can happen. Concurrently with increasing awareness, you need to build safeguards that can protect you even when willpower fails. You need hard limits, maximum number of trades per ticker, maximum daily loss per name, maximum number of failed attempts before you walk away. Ideally, loss limits are hard coded into your software or at your broker. Professional traders do not rely on willpower alone because willpower disappears once emotions take over. Next, remove sources of bias whenever possible. If certain Twitter accounts trigger you into emotional trading, mute them. When you truly internalize the process, you do not care about any of those things. You only care about taking trades per your system. Now, let me address an area of overtrading that is purely physiological and almost never discussed by anyone out there. You need to understand that overtrading becomes dramatically worse when you are not mentally sharp. Poor sleep, stress, distractions, sickness, and interpersonal issues all lower your emotional discipline significantly. Some of my worst trading decisions ever came on days where I was mentally compromised before the opening bell even rang. One of the simplest but most powerful rules I ever developed was this. If I'm not physiologically at my best, I reduce risk dramatically or I don't trade at all. And listen, you're never going to eliminate overtrading completely. Human beings are emotional. Discretionary trading is hard. There will always be moments where greed, frustration, boredom, or ego sneak back in. The goal is not perfection. The goal is reducing unforced errors as much as possible over time.
