Title: Study These 2 Types of IPOs for Trading #trading #IPO #tradingstrategy
URL: https://youtube.com/watch?v=i42Kph6hy9o
======================================================================

An IPO stands for initial public offering. This is when a private company sells shares to the public for the first time and begins trading on a stock exchange like the NYSE or Nasdaq. Before that moment, the company shares are owned by insiders, founders, employees, venture capital firms, and private investors. When a company decides to go public, investment banks step in to underwrite the deal. Their job is to determine a valuation, set an IPO price, and distribute shares to institutional clients like hedge funds. This is where things get interesting. When you hear that a company is pricing their IPO at $30 per share, most beginner traders assume that means the stock will open around $30. That is almost never what actually happens. The IPO price is simply the price that institutions pay in the offering, but those shares don't immediately trade on the open market. Instead, the exchange waits until enough buy and sell orders build up to establish the first trade in a rough price equilibrium. That's why IPOs often open far above their official IPO price. Sometimes, as we'll see, way above. You can see a company price at 30, open at $55 or $70 or even higher. That gap exists because demand exceeded supply, which leads us to the concept of oversubscribed versus under-subscribed IPOs. An oversubscribed IPO means there were far more buyers than shares available. Institutional funds all want allocations. Hedge funds want in. Long-only funds want exposure, and everyone is left scrambling for a piece of what ends up being a small limited deal. When that happens, demand spills over into the open market, and the stock often opens significantly higher than the IPO price. An under-subscribed IPO is the opposite. Demand is weak, institutions aren't fighting for shares, maybe the valuation is too high, or maybe the company isn't exciting, or maybe it's just the overall market environment isn't great for an IPO. In those cases, the stock can open near or even below its IPO price. And those are usually much harder trades.
