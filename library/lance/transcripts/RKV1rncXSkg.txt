So what is reading the tape? The term reading the tape goes back to the days of Jesse Livermore, from the famous book Reminiscences of a Stock Operator. Back in the day, there was a ticker tape that would print out the time and sales information for every transaction that would occur in each stock. Traders would use this information to know the price of the stock and create stock charts with it. The phrase reading the tape refers to traders using the size and direction of transactions to infer knowledge about large buyers and sellers, and bet on which price the stock would go next as a result. Of course, much has changed since then with the advent of electronic trading. Nowadays, reading the tape means using what's called the level two box and the ECN book, along with the time and sales window, also known as the prints window. You use these to discern useful information about the price action of the stock. So what are all these things? Allow me to explain. A level two window is a real-time display of the current buy and sell orders sitting in the market at different price levels. It shows which market makers or ECNs, electronic communication networks like Nasdaq or Arca, are bidding or offering shares, the prices they are quoting, and the size available at each price level. Traders use it to gauge short-term supply, demand, liquidity, and potential support or resistance areas. Always on a level two box, you will have the bids on the left side with the highest bid at the top. Below the top bid, you will have less competitive bids displayed in descending order below. And on the right side, you will have the offers or the ask with the lowest price at the top. Below the top offer, you will have less competitive offers displayed in ascending order below. The top line of both the bid and the offer make up what is referred to as a national best bid and offer or NBBO. Those prices represent the best price at which you could buy or sell the instrument. Keep in mind, this level two box only displays visible liquidity. We will get into more on that later. Another important aspect to keep in mind, which takes some getting used to, is that all of the size displayed on these windows are in terms of lots. One lot represents 100 shares. If you see the number five next to the bid, it represents 500 shares, not just five. A time and sales window, often called the tape, shows the actual trades that have already occurred and displays them chronologically. Even dark pool transactions, which are transactions that happen privately off the public exchange, often when institutional participants want to hide their size. The data of the transactions in this window provides the exact price, size, and time of each transaction as they happen. While the level two shows resting orders that may or may not execute, time and sales shows the actual transactions that occurred. An ECN book, meanwhile, unlike the level two, shows the full order book from a specific electronic communication network or ECN, such as Arca, EdgeX, or Nasdaq. An ECN is really just an electronic marketplace that matches buy and sell orders from traders, institutions, and market makers. Much of modern market liquidity is displayed through these networks. While the level two mainly shows the best bids and asks across multiple venues, the ECN book provides much deeper market depth through what is called depth of book, revealing many more price levels above and below the current market price. This allows traders to see deeper layers of liquidity and larger resting orders sitting further away from the inside market. If you look at this ECN book, you are able to see all the Arca orders, and Nasdaq orders, and EdgeX orders, both bids and offers. This differs from the level two box, which is just the best bid and offer from each venue. Utilizing these tools in conjunction, you can gauge how aggressive are the buyers versus sellers. Are there any notably large buyers or sellers? Are the bids or offers thicker and more liquid? How easily can I get into or out of the stock to help me gauge what slippage might be involved? We are going to make reading the tape as easy as reading a Dr. Seuss book. Except minus all those big weird words like snazzberries or something. >> The snazzberries taste like snazzberries. >> Let me explain first a couple different types of buyers and sellers. First, buyers and sellers can show all of their size. Meaning if they want to buy 5,000 shares, you will see a bid for 50 lots. Remember, all of the windows being discussed always display shares in terms of lots. One lot is 100 shares, so when you see 50, it really represents 5,000 shares. Buyers and sellers can also use a refreshing order, also known as an iceberg order. Refreshing orders are orders that repeatedly reload after getting partially or fully executed. For example, a trader may only display 1,000 shares on the level two, even though they actually want to buy 100,000 shares. Every time the visible 1,000 shares get hit, another 1,000 immediately appears again at the same price. Traders watch for this because it can signal a large institutional buyer or seller absorbing supply or demand. Hidden orders are orders that do not publicly display their size at all on the order book. They still exist and can execute, but other traders cannot fully see them on the level two. You often detect hidden orders indirectly when traders keep executing at a price level despite little or no visible size being shown there. Both refreshing and hidden orders are commonly used by large players trying to avoid revealing the full size of their position to the market. That being said, most retail traders have access to all of these order types. Especially if you are using proper trading software, you should be able to choose your order venue, as well as have options for hidden orders or refreshing orders. Reading the tape, which in modern times is a conjunction of analyzing the level two box along with the prints window, allows us to better identify how large or players are buying or selling stock. Let's first highlight what an offer showing size would look like if someone bought the full amount of size. If we see an Arca offer for 25,000 shares at $15, and then we see the offer disappear along with a P 250 $15 print, we can infer that someone paid the Arca offer, which is the exchange code P, and bought the full 25,000 shares for $15. What would it look like if we had a refreshing buyer of 25,000 shares at $15 on EdgeX? The buyer might only be displaying 400 shares at a clip. However, each time the 400 share bid decrements, it would refresh and redisplay its 400 shares until the 25,000 shares are all bought. This would be noticed on the tape by seeing repeated transactions on EdgeX at $15 that far exceed the quantity displayed. Finally, we will go over hidden orders. Hidden orders are not seen on the level two box or book, but you can see the footprint of their existence on the prints window. Despite their not being a visible order on the book, see orders being transacted through the time and sales at a certain price and on a specific venue. This is similar for dark pool orders, which can be transacted with using certain venues, but are not displayed on the level two book or box. Every order type has a different level of aggression depending on how urgently the trader wants execution versus price. A passive order is something like placing a buy limit order below the current market price. You are essentially saying, "I only want to buy if the stock comes to me." You are prioritizing price over execution. So, this is not that aggressive. A slightly more aggressive action would be improving the current bid. For example, if the best bid is $50 and you bid $50.07, you are stepping in front of other buyers to increase your odds of getting filled. You still care about price, but you are showing more urgency. More aggressive still is taking the offer, meaning you actively buy at the current ask price. Instead of waiting for sellers to come to you, you will cross the spread and immediately take liquidity from the market. The most aggressive order is a market order, where you effectively say, "Get me in now at whatever price is available." Here, execution matters more than price. Reading the tape is largely about judging the relative aggressiveness of buyers and sellers. Are buyers repeatedly lifting offers? Are sellers aggressively hitting bids? Are the bids moving higher and pushing price upward? Or are offers coming lower? The market is constantly revealing which side is more urgent and emotional. Strong bullish tape action often looks like buyers repeatedly taking offers, bids refreshing higher, sellers unable to push the stock down, and price quickly reclaiming pullbacks. Bearish tape action often looks like bids collapsing, sellers aggressively hitting the bids, failed bounces, and offers constantly moving lower. One of the best exercises for developing tape reading skills is watching recordings of major market moments. These could be panic sell-offs, breakouts, short squeezes, earnings reactions, or breaking news events, and narrating the price action in real time. For most of my career, I would record my trading screen using software like Camtasia or OBS. At the end of the trading day, I would cut the recording down to the most essential couple of minutes. Then, after hours, I'd rewatch the key moments of the trading day, narrating what I was watching and rating the price action on a scale of -10 to +10. -10 represents complete panic and capitulation. Sellers are emotional, desperate, and highly aggressive. Buyers are backing away, and liquidity is disappearing. Zero represents balance and neutrality. Neither buyers nor sellers have a meaningful edge. The stock is trading in a relatively normal, balanced state without strong emotional pressure. Positive 10 represents euphoric bullishness. Buyers are highly aggressive, repeatedly lifting offers, chasing price higher, and overwhelming the available supply. This narration process forces you to think in terms of pressure, urgency, psychology, and quality of movement, rather than just staring at candles mechanically. Over time, you begin recognizing the difference between weak moves, healthy trends, exhaustion, panic, and true institutional aggression. The first challenge for you to improve your reading the tape game is to spend even just 10 to 20 minutes each trading day after hours reviewing tape from a very in-play moment. Maybe that day there was a nice breaking news headline or a panic or a really great breakout. Or maybe this is a historical session that you've taken note of. The tape could be from trading that you recorded yourself using a program like OBS, or you could use TradingView. But what I want you to do is start to observe the dynamics of the level two. Begin to learn to connect the time and sales transactions to what you are seeing on the level two box. Can you use those tools to start to tell a story of what transpired?
