For stocks, that usually means studying how much money the company makes and what the future prospects of the company's earnings will be. So, does a knowledge of a company's fundamentals help a trader make better decisions? As with most things, the answer is more nuanced than a straight yes or no. If I had to generalize, in my experience, fundamentals do not help active traders nearly as much as people think. In fact, fundamental analysis often does more harm than it helps. What? Not because fundamentals are useless, but because they tend to get used in a way that makes traders stubborn, late, or biased. The first reason why I find that fundamentals hurt most traders is that most traders don't have the required skill to have an edge using fundamental analysis. If you're trading stocks, you're competing with sector specialists who have followed an industry for a decade, analysts with networks and management access, teams building detailed models with channel checks and alternative data, and people who understand the nuances of accounting. The issue with most traders using fundamental analysis is the same issue that most analysts using technical analysis would have. You can't just blindly say a stock is a buy because it is above a moving average. The same way you can't say a stock is cheap just because it has a low PE ratio. A lot of fundamental analysis from traders is really fundamental vibes. A metric or a narrative or even a Twitter thread that gives traders a false sense of confidence that a stock is overvalued or a great company. Often for traders, a false sense of confidence is one of the most dangerous mindsets you can ever have. You short something because it's clearly overvalued, yet then it goes higher for two more weeks. And instead of managing risk, you start to rationalize. I'm early, I'm not wrong. The second way that fundamentals become dangerous for traders is that time frame mismatch. Most traders operate over very short time frames. Fundamentals often resolve over quarters or years. The long run can be a very long time away when a stock is squeezing against you. A stock can be fundamentally absurd and still go up another 50% before it collapses. And if you're short before it's absurd, that belief does nothing to protect you from market pain, forced covering, or blowing your account. Fundamentals become a someday argument while price is acting today, and your trading needs to happen today as well. Let me give you an example of how this can spell danger for traders. Fundamentals can also help you understand positioning and who the participants are.
