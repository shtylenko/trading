Many traders are celebrating the potential removal of the PDT rule like it's universally bullish for [music] retail traders, but I think people need to think about this more carefully because the impact completely depends on which group you belong to. For skilled professional traders, this is probably a positive. More access means more unskilled flow entering the market, and unskilled flow is opportunity for professionals. It's the same as a professional poker player sitting at a table where suddenly a bunch of inexperienced players are allowed to play bigger and more often. Now, for serious aspiring traders, I can also see the argument that this is a net positive. Markets become more accessible, people can learn with smaller accounts, and there are fewer artificial restrictions. But even then, people should understand something. Access is not edge. Just because [music] you can trade more frequently doesn't mean you should. Most developing traders would still be far better off refining edge in demo before risking real capital. And then there's the largest group of all, the normies. This is a group I genuinely worry about because trading is incredibly hard. Most people are not going to develop edge. They're going to gamble in markets and piss away money. And we're already seeing this trend everywhere. Sports betting, prediction markets, funded trader programs, zero-day options, it's all gambling. An entire generation increasingly feels like traditional wealth creation is cooked, so they're gravitating toward high-risk shortcuts. Most people should not be day trading. [music] And ironically, removing barriers may ultimately transfer even more money from struggling retail traders to already skilled professionals. That's the part nobody wants to acknowledge.
