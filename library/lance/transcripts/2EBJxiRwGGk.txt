The first lesson I have for you all was also my most costly lesson this year. The biggest mistake I made was not having enough cash on hand as collateral in my trading account. Having worked for a firm most of my career, I hadn't yet had to learn this lesson. Every day I showed up and I could rely upon my buying power being there and even having more if I needed it. In my trading account, pretty much all of the collateral is long-term equity holdings. Boring stuff like SPY, but also some longer-term single stock holdings. And during the sell-off, those long-term holdings got hit hard, really really hard. So, while the market was offering some of the best trading opportunities of the entire year, panic, dislocation, forced selling, insane risk reward, my long-term book going down meant my buying power got crushed. At a time when I wanted as much buying power as possible, I actually ended up trading off my back foot and needing to factor in a whole other layer of complex decisions that I was not used to. Do I sell my long-term equity holdings and SPY for more buying power? How do I sell this stuff that is down 30% or more when it's so likely to bounce? What if I sell all these holdings, then I'm not at my desk for that one Trump tweet, and I miss the whole market bounce anyways? Because when the market sells off, your investments don't just lose value, they steal your ability to deploy capital into the best expected value setups of the year. The real cost isn't the drawdown on the long-term holdings, but the opportunity cost of being handcuffed during the moments where EV, or expected value, is the highest. Additionally, I never wanted to be margin called and my trading takes up a ton of margin. As a result, I was locking in losses to reduce exposure while simultaneously swinging the bat a fraction as hard as I would have wanted. The end result, this mistake, between losses and opportunity cost, probably could have cost me close to $10 million. Lesson learned, I need to always be thinking about my underlying capital because it is often during those moments when you lack the buying power that you want it most. As a result, ever since then, I've been reducing investments across the board and building up liquidity in my portfolio. My advice to you is to reflect on your collateral and stress test it against various market scenarios, making sure you will always have enough ammo to trade when it's best rather than being forced into defense. You need to take the time to study what the market is calling for and build that new playbook. Collaboration with other traders will help you do so. They become emotionally attached to being one type of trader and it becomes their trader identity. I'm a momentum guy. I'm a small cap trader or I'm an IPO trader or a swing trader. Lesson five, this move has been apparent to me for a while and I've been tweeting the highlights in the news, but the move to 24/7 trading is coming and it's coming fast. I don't know, but I do know traders will need to adapt. There is one implication I'm sure of. As markets increasingly go 24/7, it will become more and more important for traders to set boundaries. What opportunities might you be willing to trade during weekends? Does it need to be a $1,000 trade or a $10,000 trade? Many know how much I care about my health and peace of mind. Yet now with Blue Ocean and equities trading nearly 24 hours a day, it has made my move toward swing trading far more costly in terms of lifestyle. There were so many days these past few years that I've barely slept while monitoring exposure or trading in and out of positions.
