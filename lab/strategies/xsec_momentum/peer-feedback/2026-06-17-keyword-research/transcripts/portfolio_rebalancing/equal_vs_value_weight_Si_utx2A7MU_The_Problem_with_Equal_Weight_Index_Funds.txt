One of the most persistent questions I receive from viewers on this channel revolves around equal weight index funds or some variation of that inquiry. I can assure you the reality of equal weight index funds is far less revolutionary than it might seem. But because the question continues to come up, it's time to address it. Market capitalization waiting, which is the standard methodology for typical index funds, assigns portfolio weights based on the relative size of each company. An equal weight index fund, as its name suggests, allocates the same percentage to every stock within a given market or market segment. At first glance, equal weighting appears to be the perfect solution to many of the perceived problems plaguing market cap weighted indexes today, such as high valuations and significant concentration in a handful of top stocks. Historically, equal weighting has also outperformed cap weighting over several decades. However, this approach introduces its own set of unique risks, costs, and inefficiencies. I'm Ben Felix, chief investment officer at PWL Capital, and in this video, I'm going to explain why I do not use equal weight index funds in our portfolios. Right now, equal weight index funds do look particularly compelling. They avoid the heavy concentration found in a market cap weighted fund and boast a long track record of strong performance. even when looking at live fund data that reflects real world fees and costs. For example, the Invesco S&P 500 equal weight ETF has outperformed the standard S&P 500 for most of its existence since 2003, including the full period since its inception. Though, it's worth noting that the margin of victory is quite slim. But before we get too excited, it's critical to understand the tradeoffs and to place this past performance in its proper context. I will also demonstrate why I believe the benefits of equal weighting can be achieved more efficiently without its associated drawbacks. Let's begin with a quick overview of equal weighted versus market capitalization weighted indexes. When constructing a stock index, one of the most fundamental decisions is how to weight the constituent stocks. Market capitalization waiting means holding companies in proportion to their total market value. Market capitalization is simply what the entire company is worth on the stock market. The cost to buy every single outstanding share of a company like Apple or Shopify. In this model, a larger company naturally constitutes a larger portion of the index. It makes intuitive sense. Equal waiting, on the other hand, assigns an identical weight to every stock regardless of its market capitalization. These two approaches can result in dramatically different portfolios even when starting with the same universe of stocks. The logic behind market capitalization or market cap waiting is straightforward. The market itself has already determined how much weight each stock should have. This is why market cap index investing is often referred to as passive. You are passively accepting the market's collective judgment on the relative value of each company. It's akin to letting water find its own level rather than constantly trying to adjust it yourself. A major advantage of this approach is that it doesn't require you to make active decisions about waiting. The weights simply are what they are. Another benefit is that because the index mirrors the market, it doesn't need frequent rebalancing. As stock prices fluctuate, a market cap weighted index fund evolves in lock step with the market, requiring minimal trading. One of the perceived problems with market cap waiting is that it can lead to high concentrations in a relatively small number of stocks. We are seeing an extreme version of this in the U s market right now at least relative to its own history. This kind of index concentration es and flows naturally over time as certain businesses become exceptionally successful and command a larger share of the market. While market concentration can seem concerning, the historical relationship between concentration levels and future market returns has not been strong either in the US S or in other countries around the world. If we sort 10-year future U stock stock market returns based on their starting level of market concentration, there really isn't much of a discernable pattern. Many other countries have been and are today more concentrated in their top stocks than the U S market currently is even at its historically high levels. Regardless, index concentration remains a prominent concern for many investors. An equal weight index by its very design will never become concentrated in a few stocks in the way a market cap index can. However, it will result in significant underweights and overweights of individual stocks relative to their market cap weights. This raises an interesting question. Is the high weight in large companies within a market cap portfolio riskier than the large overweights to smaller companies and underweights to larger companies in an equal weight portfolio? The answer isn't black and white, but I think a legitimate argument can be made that the extreme over and underweing in an equal weight portfolio is actually riskier than accepting concentration in larger companies within a capweighted portfolio. This risk manifests in a couple of different ways. One is volatility. If we compare an ETF tracking the S&P 500 to one tracking the S&P 500 equal weight index, the 15-year standard deviation, a measure of the variability of returns, is quite a bit higher for the equal weight index. But volatility isn't the only way to think about risk. Another way is to measure exposure to certain types of stocks that tend to move together. Some stock categories like small cap and value stocks are theoretically priced by the market as being riskier. An equal weight index has greater exposure to these types of stocks than a market cap weighted one simply because smaller and cheaper stocks make up a smaller portion of the overall market. Equal weighting all stocks therefore results in a natural tilt towards smaller and lower price companies. This brings us to another one of the perceived benefits of equal waiting in the current market environment. Stock valuations in the U. S market are high relative to their own history and high current valuations have historically been associated with lower future returns. While not perfectly predictive, there is a relationship there. Due to its natural tilt away from the largest and highest price stocks, an equal weight index will have a lower aggregate valuation than a market cap weighted index. We can see this today with the price to earnings and price to book ratios for an equal weight S&P 500 fund being much lower than those for its market cap weighted counterpart. Exposure to smaller and cheaper stocks is not inherently a bad thing. In fact, I would argue that it's a good thing when implemented correctly. The problem is that if you want this exposure, there are probably more efficient ways to get it. I'll explain that in a moment. Equal weight indexes will also tend to have materially different sector exposures compared to market cap indexes. These sector over or underweights introduce another type of risk that most investors likely want to avoid. Again, looking at the pair of S&P 500 ETFs, we can see their sector compositions are substantially different with the equal weight index having a large underweight to technology and a significant overweight to industrials. If you intentionally seek those exposures because you have a strong view on future sector performance, I suppose that's fine. But successful sector betting tends to be extremely difficult. A lesserk known issue with equal waiting involves the implications for rebalancing. One issue is the need for more frequent rebalancing. When a smaller company's stock price rises, a market cap index doesn't need to do anything. But an equal weight index will likely need to sell down that position at its next rebalancing to restore the equal weights. The reverse happens when a large company gets smaller. As a result, the S&P 500 equal weight index ETF has exhibited an average annual turnover more than 10 times higher than a comparable market cap weighted S&P 500 ETF. This turnover comes with both implicit and explicit trading costs which are ultimately borne by the funds investors. The other rebalancing issue is that equal waiting is inherently a negative momentum strategy. This is a big deal. So, let me explain what it means. Momentum is the extremely welldocumented phenomenon first identified by Jugadish and Titman in their landmark 1993 study that stocks that have recently performed well tend to continue performing well for a period while those that have recently done poorly tend to continue underperforming to maintain their equal allocations. Equal weight funds are always selling what has done well recently and buying what has done poorly. If a stock performs well and its value increases, its weight will exceed the equal weight target. If a stock performs poorly, its weight will fall below the target. Consequently, maintaining equal weights means systematically trading against momentum. We can demonstrate this with a multiffactor regression, which is a statistical test that reveals the underlying drivers of a fund's returns. It's like a blood test for a fund. It tells you what's in your system. Looking again at the equal weight S&P 500 ETF, we can see that it loads positively on the size factor and the value factor, but loads negatively on the momentum factor. These factor loadings are all statistically significant, meaning they represent real patterns, not just random noise in the data. This confirms that the fund has more exposure to small cap and value stocks than the total market, and that it is trading in the opposite direction of momentum. This is exactly what you would expect based on the index's structure. It is naturally overweing smaller, cheaper stocks and underweing larger, more expensive ones and its rebalancing mechanism systematically trades against momentum. Now, here is the critical question. If you desire exposure to smaller and lowerric stocks, is equal waiting the most efficient way to achieve it? Regarding the concentration concern, by tilting away from larger and higher price stocks, the dimensional fund does reduce concentration, but not to the same extreme extent as equal waiting. Exposure to smaller and cheaper stocks along with negative momentum exposure in the case of the equal weight fund explains most of the results. Equal weight indexing seems appealing right now because high concentration in top stocks and elevated market valuations in the U s are making investors nervous. That combined with some attractive long-term back tests appears to make a strong case for equal waiting. But it comes with significant baggage. Equal waiting results in large over and underweights at both the individual stock and sector levels, high portfolio turnover, and a systematic bet against momentum. The strong past returns of equal waiting are largely explained by its tilts towards smaller and lowerpric stocks, not by some inherent magic in the waiting scheme itself.
