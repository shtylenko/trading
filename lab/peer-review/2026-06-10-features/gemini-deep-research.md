# **Peer Review: Algorithmic Design and Feature Engineering of the Stocks-in-Play ORB v2 (o03) Strategy**

## **Executive Summary**

The "Stocks-in-Play Opening Range Breakout v2" (ORB v2) strategy represents an ambitious and sophisticated endeavor to systematically capture intraday institutional momentum. Built upon the foundational framework established by Zarattini, Barbon, and Aziz (2024), the core thesis relies on identifying abnormal opening volume—indicating stocks that are "in play"—and trading the subsequent directional breakout1. The transition from the original stop-order entry at the opening range high to a maker-style pullback limit entry represents a profound structural evolution. In theory, this modification flips the expected risk multiplier (![][image1]) from a negative expectation to a positive one.  
However, the empirical performance characteristics of the v2 strategy reveal critical vulnerabilities. The massive divergence between research out-of-sample (OOS) claims (+183% annualized) and engine backtest reality (\~68R annualized with a severe −61.8% drawdown) exposes fundamental flaws in how market microstructure, order execution, and validation methodologies are modeled5. Furthermore, the extreme daily variance (standard deviation of 6.9R), the severe tail-dependence where a mere 1.8% of trades generate 447% of the total profit, and a machine learning classification area under the curve (AUC) of 0.61 indicate that the current feature space and objective functions are sub-optimal.  
This comprehensive review dissects the ORB v2 algorithm across three primary domains: Algorithm Design, Feature Engineering, and Methodology. By transitioning from discrete, naive technical assumptions to continuous, mathematically rigorous, microstructure-aware frameworks, the analysis provides a detailed roadmap for reconciling the backtest discrepancy, stabilizing the variance, and maximizing the predictive edge of the machine learning ensemble.

## **Part A: Algorithm Design**

The algorithm's execution mechanics and risk management protocols govern its realized profitability far more than the underlying selection model. The known issues—specifically the 30% no-fill rate, the catastrophic performance decay in mean-reverting environments, and the massive simulation-to-production gap—demand immediate structural interventions.

### **A1: Structural Changes to the Strategy**

The current algorithmic design utilizes static, daily-derived parameters applied to a highly dynamic, non-stationary intraday environment. To address the severe variance and the research-to-engine performance gap, structural modifications across the execution and risk management layers are required.  
The first required structural change involves the rectification of the backtester's limit order fill assumptions. The 2.5–3× profit-and-loss (PnL) discrepancy between the research simulation and the engine backtest is almost certainly a symptom of optimistic limit order modeling. In a live limit order book (LOB), limit orders are subject to severe adverse selection8. A passive limit order is most likely to be filled precisely when the short-term price momentum has reversed against the trader—a phenomenon known as negative drift6. If the research environment assumes a limit order is filled the moment the bid price touches the limit price, it is capturing false liquidity. It is imperative to enforce a strict "trade-through" execution model in the backtesting engine. A buy limit order must only be registered as filled if the subsequent bar's Low is strictly less than the limit price minus one tick size. The expected impact of this change is high, as it will instantly compress the research performance to match the engine reality. The implementation cost requires approximately 50 lines of code (LOC) modifications to the event-driven backtester's exchange matching logic, with the primary pitfall being a painful but necessary reduction in simulated historical returns.  
The second structural modification addresses the stop-loss mechanism. The current algorithm employs a fixed stop-loss calculated as ![][image2]. The Average True Range (ATR) utilized here is a daily metric smoothed via Wilder's moving average. Applying a daily volatility metric to an intraday phenomenon ignores the established intraday volatility smile, where volatility is exponentially higher during the first hour of trading and decays into the midday doldrums4. Using a static daily ATR fraction results in stops that are too tight during the opening volatility expansion, causing premature stop-outs, and stops that are unnecessarily wide later in the session. The strategy must transition to an intraday volatility decay model. By calculating the historical average of the 5-minute true range specifically during the 09:30–10:00 window, the risk unit (![][image1]) can be aligned with the actual variance experienced during the trade's active lifespan. This adjustment carries a medium expected impact and a medium implementation cost (requiring intraday panel data roll-ups, \~100 LOC), but perfectly aligns risk parameters with actual market mechanics.  
The third structural change necessitates the implementation of a regime-filtering mechanism. The strategy's collapse in September 2024 (−38.4R) highlights a systemic vulnerability to mean-reverting tapes. Opening Range Breakout strategies bleed capital when intraday follow-through fails. The algorithm requires a pre-trade market regime filter to scale exposure. This can be achieved by calculating the short-term autocorrelation of 5-minute returns on a broad market proxy (such as the SPY or QQQ ETFs) over the preceding three sessions. A statistically significant negative autocorrelation coefficient flags a mean-reverting regime. Under such conditions, the algorithm should systematically reduce its leverage cap from 4x to 1x, or entirely suspend the strategy for the session. The implementation cost is low (\~30 LOC, referencing broad index data), but the expected impact is very high, serving as a critical drawdown-mitigation mechanism.

### **A2: Evaluation of Two-Tier Entry Mechanics**

The strategy currently faces a 30% no-fill rate on its passive limit entry, meaning the algorithm systematically misses the most explosive, fastest-moving equities that simply do not pull back. The proposition of adopting a two-tier entry—deploying a limit order first, with a market order fallback at the Time-To-Live (TTL) expiry—must be evaluated through the lens of execution costs.  
Converting an unfilled limit order to a market order after a 30-minute delay introduces catastrophic adverse selection5. By the time the TTL expires, the underlying asset has likely extended significantly beyond the opening range. Executing a market order at this exhausted stage implies buying the absolute top of a momentum burst, guaranteeing massive slippage and drastically reducing the distance to the ![][image3] profit target while widening the absolute distance to the initial stop-loss. This structurally corrupts the positive expectancy of the system.  
Instead of a time-based market order fallback, the optimal solution is a momentum-conditional tiered entry. This involves analyzing the order flow and relative volume *during the exact minute* of the opening range breach. If the volume transacted during the 1-minute breach candle exceeds three standard deviations of the asset's normal 1-minute opening volume, the institutional buying pressure is deemed absolute. In this specific scenario, the algorithm bypasses the passive limit order entirely and immediately crosses the spread with a marketable limit order (priced a few cents above the ask to guarantee a fill while capping extreme slippage). If the breakout volume is merely average, the system reverts to the standard passive pullback limit order. This captures the extreme right-tail outliers without falling victim to the adverse selection of a delayed market order. The expected impact is high, requiring moderate implementation cost (\~80 LOC and tick/volume data dependency at the trigger timestamp).

### **A3: Skip/No-Fill Decision Modeling**

The LightGBM classifier currently predicts the probability of an eventual ![][image3] outcome, ignoring the execution path required to achieve it. The 30% no-fill rate complicates this, raising the architectural question of whether to utilize a nuisance classifier or a 3-class output model to handle unfilled signals.  
A 3-class formulation (labeling outcomes as Stop-Fill, Target-Hit, or No-Fill) mathematically forces the decision trees to identify feature splits that segregate all three states simultaneously. However, the underlying data-generating processes for these outcomes are completely divergent. The macroeconomic and fundamental features that dictate whether a stock possesses the momentum to run ![][image3] over an entire session are entirely separate from the microstructural features (such as tick volatility or bid-ask spread) that dictate whether a limit order will execute during a brief 30-minute window10.  
Therefore, treating the no-fill outcome via a two-stage nuisance classifier is vastly superior. The architecture should be bifurcated: Model A (the Execution Head) predicts ![][image4] using purely microstructural and short-term liquidity features. Model B (the Directional Head) predicts ![][image5] using broader structural, relative volume, and macro features. This decouples the execution friction from the directional alpha, allowing each model to specialize. The impact is high, stabilizing the objective function of the primary directional model. The cost involves training and deploying two separate LightGBM instances and harmonizing their predictions (\~150 LOC).

### **A4: Variance Reduction for Tail-Driven Strategies**

The strategy exhibits an extreme daily standard deviation of 6.9R. While traditional finance relies on mean-variance optimization, such frameworks fail completely when applied to heavy-tailed, highly skewed return distributions typical of momentum breakout systems11.  
To control variance without amputating the right tail of profitability, the sizing algorithm must transition from fixed-fractional risk (![][image7] account risk per trade) to Entropic Value at Risk (EVaR) scaling. EVaR is a coherent risk measure that provides the tightest mathematical upper bound for both Value at Risk (VaR) and Conditional Value at Risk (CVaR)11. By sizing positions inversely to the EVaR of the asset's specific historical intraday drawdowns, the algorithm naturally allocates less capital to highly erratic, unstable assets and more capital to assets demonstrating smooth, persistent trends.  
Furthermore, the strategy must implement cross-sectional correlation caps during the top 10 selection process. "Stocks-in-Play" often cluster around specific macroeconomic themes or sectors (e.g., a semiconductor rally led by NVDA and AMD). If the LightGBM model selects eight highly correlated semiconductor stocks in a single session, the portfolio is not carrying eight independent 1% risk units; it is carrying a concentrated 8% risk exposure to a single sector factor. The selection engine must calculate a rolling 14-day correlation matrix among the universe of triggered stocks. If a highly ranked candidate exhibits a correlation ![][image8] with an already-selected candidate, its selection probability is penalized, forcing the model to diversify across independent alpha streams. The implementation cost for EVaR optimization and correlation capping is moderately high (\~300 LOC, requiring matrix inversion and convex optimization libraries), but the expected impact on Sharpe ratio stabilization is transformative.

## **Part B: Feature Engineering**

The current feature space relies heavily on raw price-derived metrics, yielding an inadequate AUC of 0.61. To push the model's discriminative power beyond the threshold required for robust systematic trading, the feature set must be rigorously pruned of collinear noise and enriched with orthogonal data streams representing market microstructure, relative order flow, and macroeconomic context.

The feature engineering process must target the underlying drivers of sustained intraday momentum: institutional accumulation, liquidity supply, and sector-wide catalysts.

This feature carries a high expected impact, with a medium implementation cost requiring moving covariance calculations over short intraday windows (\~60 LOC).  
**2\. Price Action: Volume Profile Point of Control (POC)** The current or\_close\_pos feature attempts to measure conviction by assessing where the 5-minute candle closed relative to its range. However, closing price alone is easily manipulated by low-volume final prints. Volume profile analysis provides a vastly superior metric by identifying exactly where the majority of institutional capital was deployed16. By disaggregating the 5-minute candle into 1-minute (or tick-level) volume bins, the algorithm can identify the Point of Control (POC)—the specific price level with the highest traded volume. A new feature, poc\_position \= (POC \- L) / (H \- L), should be engineered. If the POC is clustered near the bottom of a wide 5-minute candle but the close is at the high, it indicates that massive early selling pressure was seamlessly absorbed by institutional buyers, validating the breakout. The expected impact is high, requiring moderate implementation cost to build price-volume histograms (\~100 LOC).  
**3\. Macro Context: Overnight Treasury Yield Shifts** Equity momentum, particularly in rate-sensitive cohorts like the Nasdaq 100 or the Russell 2000, is heavily dictated by overnight movements in the bond market19. A sudden, multi-basis-point surge in the 10-year Treasury yield (TNX) overnight systematically suppresses intraday continuation in growth equities, frequently causing early opening range breakouts to trap buyers and mean-revert violently. Adding a feature that measures the overnight percentage change in the 10-year Treasury yield provides a latent macroeconomic regime indicator. This introduces a medium expected impact and a low implementation cost, requiring merely a daily data feed for TNX (\~20 LOC).  
**4\. Cross-Sectional: Sector Relative Strength** Idiosyncratic breakouts (a stock moving independently) carry different risk profiles than correlated breakouts (a stock moving with its sector). Mapping each ticker to its corresponding SPDR sector ETF (e.g., AAPL mapped to XLK, JPM mapped to XLF) allows for the calculation of relative strength16. By computing sector\_rel\_strength \= f5\_ret(Ticker) \- f5\_ret(Sector\_ETF), the model can isolate pure stock-specific catalysts from broad sector beta. A stock demonstrating high relative volume and massive outperformance against its sector ETF signals intense, focused institutional accumulation. This feature provides a high expected impact with a moderate implementation cost, as it requires a relational mapping dictionary and cross-asset data alignment (\~80 LOC).

### **B3: Harmonization of Intraday and Daily Data**

The strategy currently relies on unadjusted, raw intraday prices for execution logic, but utilizes daily ATR derived from Wilder smoothing, which generally relies on split-adjusted historical data. This bifurcated data environment introduces a severe, invisible mathematical bias.  
When a stock undergoes a corporate action, such as a 4-for-1 forward split, all historical daily adjusted prices are divided by 4 to maintain smooth continuity for technical indicators. However, if the algorithm calculates a 14-day ATR crossing over the split date using adjusted prices, and then applies that fraction to the raw, unadjusted intraday price on the current execution day, the resulting stop-loss calculation will be catastrophically distorted. The risk parameters will be entirely detached from reality, leading to infinitely wide stops or instantaneous, erroneous stop-outs.  
All feature engineering must operate within a strictly harmonized price space. The optimal solution is to calculate all historical daily metrics (ATR, average volume, gap percentages) using raw, unadjusted daily data, but strictly program the selection engine to filter out and discard any ticker that has experienced a split, dividend, or major corporate action within the 14-day lookback window. This guarantees price continuity and prevents look-ahead bias without requiring complex intraday split-adjustment recalculations on the fly. The implementation cost is low, provided the data infrastructure flags split ex-dates reliably (\~30 LOC). The expected impact is critical, as it eliminates a fatal source of systematic error.

### **B4: Managing the Constant window Feature**

The feature window=5.0 is currently hardcoded to allow the production feature vector to remain compatible with research forks investigating 3-minute or 10-minute ranges. As established, feeding zero-variance features into gradient-boosted trees incurs unnecessary computational overhead and dilutes feature importance tracking.  
The proper architectural handling of this constraint is to permanently remove window from the ![][image11] matrix during training and inference. Instead, the window length should be passed purely as structural metadata embedded within the saved model artifact (e.g., saving the model pickle file as orb\_lgbm\_v2\_5m.pkl) and defined in an external YAML configuration file. The production pipeline can then read the configuration file to determine how to aggregate the initial market data, while the model remains blind to the constant. This requires trivial implementation effort (\~15 LOC in the pipeline configuration layer) and eliminates the noise.

## **Part C: Methodology**

The validation methodologies and objective functions used to train the LightGBM model are the ultimate determinants of out-of-sample robustness. Relying exclusively on 2024 data and a binary classification label severely limits the model's ability to generalize to unseen regimes and differentiate between marginal and extreme trades.

### **C1: Walk-Forward Validation Design for Multi-Year Extension**

A strategy validated exclusively on 2024 data has only experienced a predominantly bullish, low-volatility macroeconomic environment. To extend the validation across a bear market (2022), a choppy, volatile recovery (2023), and a strong bull market (2024), a rigorous walk-forward cross-validation architecture must be engineered.  
Because the feature set relies heavily on trailing 14-day moving averages (e.g., avg\_vol\_14, ATR14), adjacent temporal data points are highly correlated. Standard k-fold cross-validation will result in catastrophic data leakage. The system requires a Combinatorial Purged Cross-Validation (CPCV) framework or a rolling walk-forward window with strict embargo periods22.

* **Training Window:** 12 months. This is required to capture full quarterly earnings cycles, seasonal volume variations, and enough market regimes to stabilize the tree splits.  
* **Purge/Gap Size:** 15 trading days. The training set ending on day ![][image12] contains information influenced by prices from ![][image13]. Therefore, a strict 15-day gap must be enforced between the end of the training period and the beginning of the out-of-sample testing period.  
* **Testing Window:** 3 months. Financial market microstructure and alpha decay evolve rapidly. Retraining the model quarterly ensures the algorithm remains dynamically adapted to recent shifts in liquidity provision and volatility, striking an optimal balance between model freshness and computational overhead.

### **C2: Label Engineering Alternatives**

The current model objective defines the label as a binary outcome: ![][image14]. This thresholded classification artificially truncates the continuous nature of financial returns, causing profound information loss. If Trade A hits ![][image15] before stopping out, it is labeled a 0\. If Trade B hits ![][image16] before stopping out, it is labeled a 1\. To the algorithm, Trade A is mathematically indistinguishable from a trade that instantly collapsed to ![][image17] upon entry. This coerces the decision trees to learn arbitrary, brittle thresholds rather than the underlying physics of momentum.  
The objective function must be transformed from binary classification to expected R regression. The target variable ![][image18] should represent the Maximum Adverse Excursion (MAE) adjusted Maximum Favorable Excursion (MFE), recorded as the continuous realized ![][image1] value at the point of the TTL limit or end-of-day exit.  
To optimize this continuous regression target, the standard Mean Squared Error (MSE) loss function is wholly inadequate, as the massive outliers (+36.8R days) will cause the gradient to explode, forcing the model to overfit to extreme historical anomalies. Instead, the model must be compiled using a Huber Loss function. Huber loss behaves as MSE for small errors (allowing for granular optimization around the mean) but transitions linearly to Mean Absolute Error (MAE) for large errors. This renders the model highly robust to the extreme right-tail trades that dictate the strategy's PnL, ensuring they pull the model's ranking hierarchy favorably without destabilizing the gradient descent. The expected impact is very high, yielding a much smoother, logically consistent ranking for the daily top 10 candidates. The implementation cost is low, requiring only parameter changes in the LightGBM configuration (objective='huber').

### **C3: Model Architecture Suitability**

An AUC of 0.61 suggests the signal-to-noise ratio within the dataset is exceptionally low. LightGBM, as an unregularized (or loosely regularized) gradient boosting framework, is prone to iteratively fitting to the residuals of financial noise. While it remains state-of-the-art for tabular data, the specific dynamics of this high-noise, low-signal environment warrant testing alternative architectures.  
In environments dominated by stochastic noise, linear models fortified with strict regularization often outperform deep ensembles by ignoring spurious, non-linear interactions that fail to generalize. Establishing a baseline using an ElasticNet Logistic Regression (incorporating both L1 and L2 penalties) is highly recommended. To capture necessary conditional logic, explicit interaction terms (e.g., multiplying rv by gap\_zscore) can be manually engineered before applying the linear model.  
If tree-based architectures are maintained, a transition to Random Forests may yield superior out-of-sample stability. Unlike boosting, which sequentially corrects errors and is highly sensitive to noise, Random Forests rely on bagging (bootstrap aggregating), which inherently reduces variance and combats overfitting. Applying extreme hyperparameter constraints—such as limiting the maximum tree depth to 3 or 4 and forcing a high min\_samples\_leaf ratio (e.g., 5% of the total dataset)—will prevent the algorithm from memorizing fragile micro-splits. The implementation cost to benchmark these alternatives is low (\~100 LOC leveraging existing data pipelines), and the expected impact is medium, primarily serving as a robustness check against the LightGBM outputs.

### **C4: Rigorous Validation via Permutation Testing**

To definitively prove whether the complex LightGBM model (with an AUC of just 0.61) provides statistically significant alpha over the naive, computationally cheap Relative Volume (RV) ranking baseline, a rigorous permutation test must be executed. Because quantitative strategy development inherently involves testing multiple features, architectures, and hyperparameters, standard Monte Carlo permutation testing is insufficient; it will succumb to multiple-testing bias and false discovery (data snooping)23.  
The gold standard for validating algorithmic trading strategies while controlling the Family-Wise Error Rate (FWER)—the probability of making even one false discovery—is the Romano-Wolf stepdown procedure25. This non-parametric methodology accounts for the correlation structure between different strategy variants, making it ideal for comparing the ML ranking against the RV ranking31.  
The implementation of the Romano-Wolf permutation test follows a strict sequence:

1. **Block Permutation of Returns:** To preserve the cross-sectional correlation of the market, raw asset prices are not shuffled. Instead, generate ![][image19] permuted datasets by shuffling the daily return vectors across the time axis. Crucially, apply the exact same permutation index across all 1,000 tickers for any given day. This destroys the temporal autocorrelation (the predictive edge) while preserving the contemporaneous correlation matrix26.  
2. **Evaluate Strategy Baselines:** Execute the full trading machinery—the naive RV-ranking strategy and the ML-ranking strategy—over all ![][image20] permuted datasets. Record the maximum performance metric (e.g., total ![][image1] or Sharpe ratio) achieved across all strategy variants for each permutation. This constructs the joint empirical null distribution, representing the maximum performance obtainable by pure random chance26.  
3. **Step-down Inference:** Compare the actual observed performance of the ML strategy against the 95th percentile of the maximum permuted performance distribution. If the observed ML performance exceeds this threshold, the null hypothesis is rejected, proving the ML edge is statistically significant and robust to data snooping30. If it fails to cross this threshold, the firm must conclude the ML model provides no reliable alpha over the RV ranking and should revert to the simpler baseline.

The expected impact of this validation is exceptionally high. It mathematically guarantees whether the engineering complexity, maintenance costs, and execution latency introduced by the machine learning infrastructure are genuinely justified. The implementation cost is high, requiring bespoke statistical programming to automate the trading engine across thousands of bootstrap iterations (\~400 LOC and substantial compute time), but it remains non-negotiable for institutional-grade strategy deployment.

## **Conclusion**

The Stocks-in-Play ORB v2 strategy rests upon a robust theoretical foundation: exploiting acute institutional liquidity imbalances at the market open. However, its current implementation is severely handicapped by optimistic execution assumptions, an inability to navigate regime shifts, and a rigid, lossy objective function that fails to capture the continuous nature of momentum returns.  
By implementing strict trade-through limit order logic, dynamically mapping stop-losses to intraday volatility decay profiles, and incorporating advanced microstructure proxies—such as volume profile Point of Control and the Roll spread estimator—the algorithm can evolve from a naive technical system into a microstructure-aware institutional execution engine. Transitioning the machine learning target from binary classification to continuous Huber-loss regression will align the selection logic with the strategy's tail-driven reality. Ultimately, executing the Romano-Wolf stepdown permutation test will definitively separate true predictive alpha from statistical noise, ensuring that the final model deployed to production possesses a mathematically verified, robust systematic edge.
