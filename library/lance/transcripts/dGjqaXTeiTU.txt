So, let's start with the basics, then get into my IPO strategies cuz there is a lot that retail traders overlook about the structure and behavior of IPOs. An IPO stands for initial public offering. This is when a private company sells shares to the public for the first time and begins trading on a stock exchange like the NYSE or Nasdaq. Before that moment, the company's shares are owned by insiders, founders, employees, venture capital firms, and private investors. When a company decides to go public, investment banks step in to underwrite the deal. Their job is to determine a valuation, set an IPO price, and distribute shares to institutional clients like hedge funds. This is where things get interesting. When you hear that a company is pricing their IPO at $30 per share, most beginner traders assume that means the stock will open around $30. That is almost never what actually happens. The IPO price is simply the price that institutions pay in the offering, but those shares don't immediately trade on the open market. Instead, the exchange waits until enough buy and sell orders build up to establish the first trade in a rough price equilibrium. That's why IPOs often open far above their official IPO price, sometimes as we'll see way above. You can see a company price at 30, open at $55 or $70 or even higher. That gap exists because demand exceeded supply, which leads us to the concept of oversubscribed versus under subscribed IPOs. An over subscribed IPO means there were far more buyers than shares available. Institutional funds all want allocations. Hedge funds want in. Long only funds want exposure. And everyone is left scrambling for a piece of what ends up being a small limited deal. When that happens, demand spills over into the open market and the stock often opens significantly higher than the IPO price. An under subscribed IPO is the opposite. Demand is weak, institutions aren't fighting for shares, maybe the valuation is too high, or maybe the company isn't exciting, or maybe it's just the overall market environment isn't great for an IPO. In those cases, the stock can open near or even below its IPO price. And those are usually much harder trades. Now, this brings up one of the most common questions beginners have. Why do IPOs tend to have such a big pop on day one? So, there's a few structural reasons, and this trend is very much real and backed by data. First, investment banks intentionally price IPOs slightly below what they believe the market will pay. This practice is known as IPO underpricing. Banks do this because their job isn't just to sell shares, it's to make sure the deal is successful. A strong first day performance makes institutional clients happy, it makes the company look good, and helps the bank secure future deals. In other words, they would rather leave a little money on the table than risk the IPO failing. Second, supply is extremely constrained on day one. Only a small portion of the company's total shares are actually available for trading. Meanwhile, demand can be massive from institutions, hedge funds, and retail traders who were not able to get an allocation. That imbalance between limited supply and overwhelming demand is what creates the initial surge. And like I said, the data backs this up. Historically, IPOs have produced significant average first day gains. Over the past few decades in the US, the average first day return has been roughly 15 to 20%. In hotter markets, it can be much, much higher. During the 2020 to 2021 bull market following the COVID panic, the average IPO pop was close to 30 to 40% on the first day due to the prevalence of market euphoria. Of course, those are averages. Some IPOs explode much more than that, while others barely move at all. Another huge factor that determines how explosive an IPO will be is the float. Float is the number of shares that are actually available for trading. In many IPOs, the float is tiny relative to demand. The company might be worth billions of dollars on paper, but only a small portion of shares are actually released to the public at first. That tight supply creates an environment where price can move extremely fast. Sector matters, too. Markets trade on narratives, especially hot IPOs. So, when a hot sector is in favor, like AI, crypto infrastructure, electric vehicles, or fintech, money often floods into anything even loosely related to that theme. If an IPO launches into a hot sector during a strong market environment, the probability of a large move increases dramatically. You saw this last year with companies like Circle and Figma. When the market is in that type of risk-on mood, IPOs can trade like absolute rockets. But when markets are choppy or bearish, IPOs tend to struggle because market participants are far more conservative with how much money they want in markets. Before I show you my strategies and examples, let's talk about lockups because this is another piece that many new traders misunderstand. When a company goes public, insiders are typically restricted from selling their shares for a period of time. This is known as the lockup period. It usually lasts around 90 to 100 days. Most often, it's 6 months or the 180 days. It tends to apply to founders, employees, early venture capital investors, institutional backers, and similar firms like that. The purpose of the lockup is to prevent early investors from immediately dumping their shares into the market. Imagine if venture capital funds could instantly sell billions of dollars worth of stock the moment a company is listed and open for trading. The price would collapse instantly. So, during the lockup period, the float is artificially restricted. Only the shares sold in the IPO are actively trading. When the lockup expires, suddenly a massive number of additional shares can hit the market. That increase in supply can create significant downward pressure on the stock, which is why lockup expirations are something traders like myself watch very closely. All right. This is one of the most powerful patterns in IPO trading because the market is still discovering the correct price.
