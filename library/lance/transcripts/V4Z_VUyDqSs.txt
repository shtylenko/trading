Continuation scalps on the other hand, occur around breaks of clean support or resistance. When scalping here, you are trying to be the first market participant to enter upon the break. So, if we have a $10 resistance level, you want to be buying $10.01 or $10.02, and scalping out on that emotional follow-through, much like the example we gave earlier. Your success in a continuation scalp trade will depend heavily on timing and confirmation from real-time order flow. Imagine a stock that has earnings this morning. Volume is five times normal. It pushes up from $48 to $50 in the first hour. Now, it's consolidating under $50, a clean psychological resistance level. You see multiple pushes into that $50. The pullbacks are shallow, and each dip finds buyers higher than the previous dip. That is attention building. On the level two box, which is again, the window displaying the bids and offers, you see that large offer sitting at $50. Now, you watch the tape. Are the buyers aggressively lifting into that offer? Are prints going off repeatedly at $50? Is that offer refreshing, meaning it's reappearing after partial fills, or is it getting taken? If buyers continue lifting and the offer size begins to shrink, then suddenly it's gone, you want to be the one buying at $50.01, if possible, and potentially kicking it out 10, 20, or even 30 cents higher. Often, your stop-loss would be right below that level if it gets back below the $50. Strong breakouts feel urgent, and you want to see the stock burst through that level with conviction. Weak breakouts drift through a level and stall. The tape slows, sellers start hitting bids just above the resistance, and that often leads to failed breaks. A continuation scalp that works well will move quickly in your favor.
