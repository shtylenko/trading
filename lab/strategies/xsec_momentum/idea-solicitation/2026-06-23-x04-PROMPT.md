# Brainstorm: new ideas to improve a Residual-Momentum equity strategy

You are a senior quantitative researcher. I run the strategy described below and want your
help finding NEW ways to improve its risk-adjusted profitability. I have already tested a
long list of variations (Section 7) — your ideas MUST NOT overlap with those. I am looking
for genuinely different angles, each concrete enough that I could backtest it.

## 1. What the strategy does

A **long-only, monthly-rebalanced US equity momentum strategy.** Once a month it ranks a
liquid universe of US stocks, buys a basket of the top names equal-weight, holds for about a
month, and repeats. There are no stops and no profit targets — the only exit is the timed
monthly rebalance.

The distinctive part is *what* it ranks on. Instead of raw past return (which quietly turns
into a leveraged bet on the overall market — the biggest "winners" in a bull market are
usually just the highest-beta names), it ranks on **market-adjusted, stock-specific
momentum.** For each stock, over an ~11-month formation window (the past 252 trading days,
skipping the most recent 21 days because that last month tends to mean-revert), I run a
one-factor regression of the stock's daily returns on the S&P 500's daily returns:

```
r_stock(t) = α + β · r_SPY(t) + ε(t)
```

`ε(t)` is the **residual** — the part of the stock's return *not* explained by the market.
The ranking score is the **residual information ratio**, `mean(ε) / std(ε)`: the average
stock-specific drift divided by its volatility. High score = strong, *steady*,
market-independent momentum. I rank all eligible stocks by this score and buy the top basket
equal-weight.

**Eligibility (feasibility gates, not signals):** last close ≥ $5; 20-day average dollar
volume ≥ $10M; at least ~273 days of history. Sector is never an input — any sector
concentration is incidental.

**Current parameters:** **top 35 names**, equal weight (~3% each), rebalance every 20 trading
days, 20-day hold, enter at the rebalance close, pure time exit. Daily split-adjusted prices;
the only "factor" used is SPY.

## 2. How it evolved to here

- **v1 — plain 12-month-minus-1-month momentum.** Buy the biggest trailing winners, top 50
  equal-weight, monthly. It works, but it's a *leveraged market bet*: book beta ≈ 1.4,
  correlation ~0.7 to the market, max drawdown −25% to −38%, and it carries momentum's
  notorious crash risk (sharp losses when prior losers violently rebound). Realistic Sharpe
  ~0.7–0.9. This version is validated end-to-end including a held-out year — it's a real but
  beta-heavy momentum tilt.

- **v2 — residual (market-neutralized) momentum, top 50.** Same idea, but rank on the
  *residual* information ratio above instead of raw return, to strip out the accidental
  market leverage. Result (in-sample over 8 years): roughly the same return family but
  materially less market risk — beta ≈ 1.08 instead of 1.4, max drawdown ~−23% instead of
  ~−36%, Sharpe ~0.89 vs ~0.81. The honest framing: **this is better-engineered momentum, not
  a new source of alpha** — the win is risk reduction. It's ~0.95 correlated with plain momentum.

- **v3 (current incumbent) — same signal, top 35.** Identical to v2 except the basket is
  trimmed from the top 50 to the **top 35**, because an exploratory study found the
  weakest-scored tail of the 50 dilutes risk-adjusted return. The improvement is small (~0.1
  Sharpe) and the optimal width is regime-unstable (one window favored ~15 names, another ~35;
  35 is the only width two independent windows agreed on). It's a marginal challenger to v2,
  not yet confirmed superior on fresh data. **Top-35 residual momentum is the version to beat.**

## 3. Current performance & honest characterization

- **Realistic Sharpe ≈ 1.0**, long-only. Book beta to market ≈ 1.08. Max drawdown ~−23%.
  Correlation ~0.95 to plain momentum.
- **This is a modest, real, long-horizon equity premium — not a high-octane strategy.** Its
  market-orthogonal alpha is thin and not strongly statistically significant; on the cleanest
  recent data the residual alpha is roughly zero (the edge shows up as *lower beta and
  shallower drawdown for the same momentum return*, i.e. risk engineering, more than as new
  alpha). On older data there's a modestly significant alpha, but that sample is partly
  survivorship-lifted.
- **Key risks:** (a) it shares momentum's regimes, including **momentum-crash risk** — sharp
  losses when beaten-down losers violently rebound (e.g. early 2009, parts of 2021–22);
  (b) **single-factor** — I strip out the market but not size/value/sector, so some
  "idiosyncratic" signal may still be a style tilt; (c) **long-only** — full market exposure
  in a downturn, no short side to hedge or harvest.

## 4. What would count as a win

Baseline is **≈ 1.0 Sharpe / ~−23% max drawdown / β≈1.08**. The last accepted refinement
(top-50 → top-35) bought only ~0.1 Sharpe, so I'm interested in:
- **(a)** a *step-change* in risk-adjusted return, OR
- **(b)** a meaningful **drawdown / crash-risk reduction** even at flat return, OR
- **(c)** a return improvement that **survives realistic trading costs.**

A marginal, regime-unstable +0.05 Sharpe is NOT worth a research cycle. Be honest about the
expected magnitude of each idea — I would rather know something is "small but robust" than be
sold a backtest mirage.

## 5. Hard constraints (ideas must respect these)

- **LONG ONLY.** No shorting, and no inverse/short ETFs or put options used as a core leg.
  (Long/short residual momentum has a higher Sharpe in theory but I cannot short.)
- **No leverage, no options.** Run at 1.0× gross.
- **Liquid US-equity universe only:** price ≥ $5 and ≥ ~$10M/day dollar volume. Going
  smaller-cap is out — clean micro-cap data is unavailable to me and is heavily
  survivorship-biased, and I separately confirmed the edge actually *decays* toward the less
  liquid names rather than strengthening.
- **Data on hand:** daily split-adjusted price bars for a broad US universe + SPY; and
  true point-in-time quarterly fundamentals (revenue, COGS, net income, assets, etc.) via SEC
  filings. I do NOT have: intraday data for this strategy, clean daily Fama-French factor
  series, analyst estimates, options surfaces, or alternative data. Prefer ideas testable on
  daily bars (+ optionally the fundamentals I have).
- **Validation discipline:** any idea must be backtestable on history (I have ~2009–2024 plus
  held-out 2025/early-2026) and must survive a walk-forward and a held-out year — I won't
  deploy something that only looks good in one regime. Cheap-to-test, structurally simple
  ideas are strongly preferred.

## 6. What I've learned about where the lever is (and isn't)

Three hard-won lessons that should focus your ideas before you even reach the kill list:
- **The RANKING SIGNAL is the lever.** Overlays on *sizing, timing, and exits* are
  structurally fragile on this strategy and repeatedly gave back the residual version's risk
  improvement (details in Section 7). Improvements have come from *what/how I rank and select*,
  not from risk-engineering on top.
- **Transaction cost is NOT the binding leak** at the capital I'd actually run — turnover is
  already low and a realistic cost model barely dents net Sharpe. So pure turnover-reduction
  ideas are low-value unless they *also* change the return.
- **The liquid-universe constraint is binding** — it is *why* every alternative long-only
  signal I've tried collapses into a high-beta tilt (the cheap, defensive, academic version of
  each factor lives in the smaller/less-liquid names I can't trade). Any new signal has to earn
  its keep *on liquid names*, not in theory.

## 7. Already tried and ruled out — DO NOT re-propose these

I've run an exhaustive search. **The central finding:** on this liquid long-only universe,
*every alternative cross-sectional ranking signal I tested collapsed into a high-beta market
tilt with essentially zero added alpha* and stayed ~0.6–0.9 correlated to the momentum book
— it did not diversify it. Separately, *every timing/sizing/exit overlay I tried was fragile
and gave back the residual strategy's risk improvement.* Specifics, grouped:

**Alternative ranking signals — all tried, all subsumed or beta-tilts:**
- Plain 12-1 total-return momentum (the predecessor) — works but beta-heavy; residual is the improvement.
- Value (book-to-market, earnings-yield, point-in-time) — failed even in 2022 (value's banner
  year); on a liquid universe high book-to-market = distressed/cyclical high-beta junk, not defensive value.
- Quality / gross-profitability (Novy-Marx GP/Assets) — looked good on raw decile spreads but
  was a high-beta asset-light-growth tilt; market-adjusted alpha ≈ 0; not additive.
- Earnings momentum (TTM net-income YoY) — borderline, breaks in chop years; secondary at best.
- 52-week-high proximity — weak, likely already captured by 12-1 momentum.
- Path quality / "frog-in-the-pan" information-discreteness (prefer smooth trends over jumpy
  ones) — subsumed by return-level momentum; residualization already trims the lottery names it targets.
- Risk-adjusted momentum (return/vol) and multi-factor (Fama-French 3-factor / size) residual
  momentum — the FF3/size residual was strictly *worse*: beta went UP, alpha went significantly
  negative. CAPM single-factor is the sweet spot; adding factors injected noise.
- Score *definition* variants (sum of residuals, residual t-stat, compounded residual return)
  and longer cadences — all tested; the `mean(ε)/std(ε)` information-ratio at a monthly cadence is the best; confirmed robust.

**Portfolio construction / weighting — tried:**
- Score-tilted weighting (overweight higher-scored names) — book too diversified to register; to
  the extent it did anything it tilted toward the highest-beta, most-crash-prone names. Equal weight is correct.
- Overlapping portfolios (Jegadeesh-Titman style) — slightly *hurt* gross return; turnover is
  already low so there was nothing to gain.
- Turnover buffer / rank hysteresis to cut churn — negligible, regime-luck-driven, flips sign across windows.
- Absolute score cutoff vs relative top-N — absolute cutoff is regime-fragile; relative top-N is right.
- Concentration sweep (how many names) — non-monotonic and window-dependent; ~35 is the only robust agreement (this is the current version).

**Sizing / leverage / vol-targeting — tried:**
- Static leverage — at this Sharpe, leverage either adds vol-drag or makes drawdowns unholdable. Run 1.0×.
- Portfolio vol-targeting (including the "done right" version scaled by the strategy's own realized
  vol) — trailing vol on monthly periods is too laggy; it de-risks near bottoms and misses rebounds. Didn't help.

**Exits / stops — tried:**
- ATR / chandelier trailing stops as an overlay — *destroyed* the strategy (whipsaws momentum
  names out at local bottoms before they recover; correlated stop-outs in vol spikes deepen drawdown).
- Fixed-percentage stops — redundant/harmful. **Pure 20-day time exit is optimal; momentum requires sitting through volatility.**

**Hedging / regime-timing / defensive overlays — tried:**
- Static beta hedge (via inverse instruments) — monotonically lowered Sharpe; beta is load-bearing for the return.
- Defensive rotation to cash/bonds/gold when the market is below its 200-day average — every
  variant lowered Sharpe AND deepened drawdown (whipsaw; bonds failed in 2022).
- Tail/VIX-triggered de-risking — high overfit risk in the trigger; this whole class is fragile here.

**Diversifier sleeves / multi-strategy blends — tried:**
- Long-only complements (low-vol, short-term reversal, near-low, short-momentum) — all positively
  correlated (shared market beta); none smoothed the book.
- Index-level short-horizon mean-reversion sleeve ("buy the dip in an uptrend" on liquid ETFs via
  IBS / RSI-2) — the dip-buys get run over as a bear opens (deeply negative through 2022); killed.
- **Conclusion reached:** no deployable long-only diversifier for this strategy exists on this universe.

**Universe changes — tried:**
- Smaller-cap / lower-liquidity tiers — the edge *decays* toward smaller names, and that data is
  survivorship-inflated, so it would look better than it is. Closed.
