Moment two for me came a few years into my career when I was just getting off the ground. I was starting to be consistently profitable and starting to even become hopeful. It was a normal quiet day at work when Allergan started ripping higher into the close. It felt like it was just some order flow into the closing imbalance. A move likely to revert back lower. I started to short into the close with the intention of holding a small position overnight. The market closes and then not long after, the stock is absolutely explodes higher in the after hours. An unfathomable move for a huge large cap pharma company. What was driving it? The news of a hostile takeover by Bill Ackman. Even by the time I had the headline, the stock was already up so much by the time I could even process it. I ended up losing over a hundred thousand dollars when my daily risk limit was something like five grand. A great month for me at the time was maybe 20K. That is months of work gone. All of a sudden you start questioning, will it take me six months to get out of the drawdown? A year? Or maybe never? How long will it take for me to get a bonus check again? So often it becomes impossible to relate to the feelings we once felt, but in that moment it was an unbelievable level of doubt whether I'd ever make it to the other side of that drawdown. Moment three came on February 5th, 2018, during what is now termed the Vix popocalypse. At this point I was almost 7 years into my career. I had made a couple million dollars and all seemed great. Going into the market close I was having one of the best days of my career. I think I was up close to a million dollars through a series of intraday trades during a volatile market day where stocks were plunging and the Vix was ratcheting higher after years of relative calm. Essentially hedge funds and institutions for years had been betting that the stock market would stay calm forever. The logic was stocks go up and any spike in volatility eventually retraces. However, on February 5th, markets sold off hard and all those short volatility positions exploded higher against them. Many of these players had leveraged their bets using inverse short volatility ETFs. So as players all raced for the exits, this led to the Vix being bought higher and higher and more and more players were caught offsides. A classic market feedback loop. And all those leveraged short volatility ETFs? Well, the price of those were all tied to the VIX futures, which closed at 4:15 p.m. Eastern time. The VIX futures ratcheted higher into that close and triggered a cascade of consequences for those ETFs. What I didn't know at the time was that the spike in volatility triggered a liquidation clause in these ETFs like the ticker XIV. So, after hours, these levered ETFs start plunging while the market wasn't even moving. The move didn't make sense to me, so I started buying up those levered ETFs. Turns out, I was the dumb day trader and I was just cannon fodder. They kept plunging and plunging and within minutes, I took my best day ever [music] and turned it into my worst day ever, down close to $20 million. The most dangerous part about mean reversion is when a trade keeps going against you, it seemingly keeps getting better and better. But in this case, I was dumb money and didn't know the key information I was missing. I was totally shell-shocked after this. It was one of the greatest emotional roller coasters of my career. To go from totally ecstatic to totally defeated, questioning once again, how long would it take me to get out of this drawdown? I was absolutely shook and I couldn't believe that in minutes my P&L swung so much money in the wrong direction. Crazily enough, another gut-check moment, moment number four, came later that year on August 9th, 2018. A boutique short report came out by a group called Rota Fortunae on a stock ticker FPI. FPI is a boring farmland REIT and the short report by Rota Fortunae argued that the company was massively overvalued, financially weak, and potentially misleading investors about its true financial condition. Because FPI just owns land, I started to play analyst, dismissing the price action and thinking, "There's no way that a simple farmland REIT could drop so much on one dumb boutique short report." This was faulty logic and poor logic, which led me to fight the move lower and that ended up being catastrophic for me. The loss at the time wasn't anything so, so huge. Moment five is special because it really represents the thousands of moments throughout my career that I felt inferior due to the comparison game. And because of that, I wanted to quit.
