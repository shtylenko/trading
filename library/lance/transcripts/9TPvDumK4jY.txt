Learning from your own wins and losses is the easiest way to find your edge. This four-step approach to performing an annual trade review has helped me make over a hund00 million in verified trading profits. The first step, get organized. If you haven't been tracking your data and categorizing your trades all year, well, you have your work cut out for you. Whether you use a trade analytics platform like Tradzella or Trader or a good old-fashioned spreadsheet, you need to aggregate all your trades and categorize them. These categories depend on your trading and what patterns you are looking to better understand. For example, you might want to analyze your swing trading versus your intraday trades or your breakouts versus your reversal trades. Step two, analyze the data looking for patterns. One of my trainees was trading a bunch of IPOs. I asked him why. He said he made money in IPOs. Factually correct, but my trainee hadn't analyzed his data deep enough. The truth was he made money in the two hot IPOs but lost money in the eight shitty IPOs. He was missing that key nuance. Essentially, he was making 20K in IPOs, but under the surface, he was really making 50K in the two hot IPOs, then giving back 30K of his profits in the eight shitty ones. Step three is to collect your 25 best and your 25 worst trades overall. The best way to do this is to screenshot both the intraday and daily charts and save them in organized notebooks or tag them appropriately. Then also write up all the trade details and P&L data. I commonly recommend that good detailed trade write-ups include much of the following details of the setup category, market environment, an analysis of the daily and intraday chart, whether there is a news catalyst, as well as what your entry, exit, and target were. You can also analyze whether you sized the trade properly, and what more nuanced factors were that might have made this trade better or worse than you first thought. Step four is to find and review any missed opportunities, trades you either didn't see or didn't take. For example, were you focused on one area of the market rather than a more opportunistic one? A lot of traders get tunnel vision. They become the small cap trader or the earnings trader or the momentum long trader. And that's fine. Specialization can be a strength until it turns into stubbornness. If the best expected value opportunities in a given month were elsewhere, well, you kept forcing the same trades that weren't working, well, then you were messing up and you were playing the wrong game.
