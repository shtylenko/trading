Title: What is MACD in Trading? #macd #trading #tradingindicators
URL: https://youtube.com/watch?v=bXMQQl4Nqf0
======================================================================

MACD stands for moving average convergence divergence. That sounds complicated and well, it turns out that MACD is indeed pretty complicated. Think about price like a runner on a track. Sometimes the runner is speeding up, sometimes the runner is slowing down. MACD is a tool that tries to measure how that runner speed is changing. Is the stock speeding up or is it slowing down? To do that, MACD uses two moving averages of price. A fast moving average that reacts quickly to new prices and a slow moving average that reacts more slowly. By default, MACD uses 12 period and 26 period exponential moving averages or EMAs. Exponential just means the most recent prices count more than older prices. So, these lines react faster than simple moving averages. The 12 period EMA is the fast view, the 26 period EMA is the slow view. Got that? Now, the main MACD line is just this. The 12 period EMA minus the 26 period EMA. If the 12 EMA is above the 26 EMA, the MACD line is above zero. That tells you the short-term price is stronger than the longer-term price and the trend is tilted up. If the 12 EMA is below the 26 EMA, the MACD line is below zero. That tells you the short-term price is weaker than the longer-term price and the trend is tilted down or flat. That zero line you see is the baseline. If above it, the bulls have the short-term momentum. Below it, the bears do. Now, let's talk about the second line, the signal line. The signal line is just a smoothed version of the MACD line. By default, it's a nine period exponential moving average of the MACD line itself. Using the runner example, the MACD line is the change in the runner's speed. The signal line smooths those changes out over nine periods. When the MACD line crosses above the signal line, it means the runner is now going faster than their recent average. When the MACD line crosses below the signal line, it means the runner is now going slower than their recent average. That's where those famous bullish crossover and bearish crossover phrases come from. Now, what about the bars, the so-called histogram? Those bars are simply showing the distance between the MACD line and the signal line. If the MACD line is far above the signal line, you get tall bars. If the MACD line is just barely above or below the signal line, you get short bars. You can think of it like this. MACD line versus signal line is how much faster or slower the runner is compared to their average. Histogram bars are a little graph of that difference. I almost tend to think of the histogram as whether the runner is accelerating or decelerating. Big bars mean strong momentum, small bars mean weak or fading momentum.
