I'm noticing investors worrying about stock market valuations being too high when stock market valuations are higher expected future returns are lower lower future returns can show up slowly over long periods of time or they can show up quickly with a stock market correction either way high valuations are not great for long term investors so to an extent the current concern is warranted but saying that stock market valuations are high misses a lot of nuance which stocks are we talking about and even more broadly which markets I'm Ben Felix portfolio manager at PWL Capital and I'm going to tell you why the stock market is not as overvalued as many people seem to think US stock market valuations are high not the highest they've ever been but still very high. typically high valuations are related to lower future returns the relationship is messy noisy and can be violated for long periods of time but it does tend to hold at least eventually high valuations have preceded major crashes in the United States and in other markets the Shiller cyclically adjusted price earnings ratio or CAPE the ratio of stock prices to smoothed ten year real earnings reached peaks in 1929 and 1999 in the US followed by major crashes valuations in Japan got outright ridiculous in 1990 leading up to a crash that the Japanese stock market is still recovering from more than 30 years later there are good reasons to be worried about current valuations which are hovering around the level that preceded the 1929 crash this doesn't mean that the market will crash tomorrow but it does suggest lower expected returns for US stocks the inverse of the CAPE ratio the Shiller earnings yield suggest a real expected return of about 3% for the S&P 500 that's not great but it's also not the whole story the expensiveness of the US market is driven by a small handful of the largest stocks using a different valuation metric the price to book ratio the US market is expensive relative to history but the market overall less so than the S&P 500 and a lot of the expensiveness is coming from the so-called Magnificent Seven Apple Microsoft Alphabet Amazon Nvidia Tesla and Meta value stocks the stocks in the market with relatively low prices compared to fundamentals don't look all that expensive in many of the historical periods where stocks with high prices have gone on to have long periods of poor performance value stocks stocks with low prices have typically perform much better for the ten year period ending February 2,010 the S&P 500 Index lost an annualized 0.31% the NASDAQ 100 lost an annualized 7.86% and the MSCI US Investable Market Value Index returned an annualized 4.56% the single worst time to retire in US stock market history was December 1968 from then until January 1984 that period over the same period the dimensional US market wide value index returned to 12.31% annualized a more extreme example is Japan from 1990 through December 2023 the Japanese stock market measured by the Dimensional Japan Market Index delivered an annualized return of 1.22% obliterating the purchasing power of an investor in Japanese stocks Japanese growth stocks fared even worse losing an annualized 0.73% over this period over the same period Japanese value stocks returned 4.27% annualized the point I'm trying to make is that while the valuations in some parts of the US market are currently high the whole US stock market does not have high valuations tilting away from US gross stocks toward US value stocks which is what ETF providers like Avantis and Dimensional Fund Advisors do makes valuations less concerning the other thing to consider is that while the US market is expensive the US is not the world international developed and emerging market stocks have much more attractive valuations than the US US stocks look invincible right now after years of dominance over international stocks but a lot of those excess returns have come from rising valuations it's not clear that US dominance will continue into the future and in terms of expected returns markets other than the US look far more attractive believing the high past returns for the US market will continue into the future without considering US valuations is something known as rear view mirror bias US stock market valuations are high leading some investors to be concerned about a crash well it is true that US market wide valuations are high a lot of that is being driven by the biggest and most expensive stocks smaller and cheaper stocks within the US market do not have concerning valuations and historically have done well when the most expensive stocks have fallen back to earth it's also important to remember that the US stock market is not the stock market international and emerging market stocks have much lower valuations and correspondingly higher expected returns a US investor with a value tilt or a globally diversified investor have fewer reasons to be concerned about high
