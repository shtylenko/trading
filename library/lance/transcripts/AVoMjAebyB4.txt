Suddenly, the sizing increases dramatically, and by the fifth or sixth trade, they're massively oversized. They're emotionally euphoric and fully convinced they can't miss. Right as the market opportunity is actually becoming crowded, overextended, and lower quality. And that is how trading cycles always catch you. Amazing opportunities in markets do not last forever. Some of the best periods I've ever traded were incredibly short windows where emotional behavior in the market created absurd opportunities. Meme stocks in 2021, certain AI momentum phases, there are times when every breakout feels like it's guaranteed to work. During these periods, opportunities are everywhere, and traders start confusing favorable market conditions with permanent personal evolutions. They think they suddenly became trading gods overnight, when in reality the market environment simply became perfectly suited to their style for a temporary period of time. Markets are adaptive. As opportunities become obvious, more traders pile in, edges compress, and the patterns become overcrowded. And then, as you increase your sizing the most, bam! The exact moment trading begins feeling easiest is often when things are becoming most dangerous underneath the surface. Don't worry, I've got the solution for you. This is why you need proactive systems in place before success starts distorting your judgment. One of the best lessons I learned from Dr. Jonathan Katz, one of the top trading psychologists in the world who I've interviewed in this video, is that great trading psychology is proactive, not reactive. Most traders wait until they are already emotionally compromised before trying to fix themselves. They wait until they're revenge trading, tilted, or emotionally exhausted before acknowledging that there's a problem. But by then, objectivity is gone, and catastrophe is lurking. The goal is to recognize when you are losing objectivity before it fully takes control of your decision-making. The first proactive step traders should take during a hot streak is awareness, especially if you have a history of overconfidence and giving it all back. I know that sounds counterintuitive, because when traders are making money, they naturally want to press harder. And yes, there are absolutely moments where extraordinary opportunity deserves extraordinary aggression. And I absolutely agree that if objectively the opportunities are still there, you do want to press. But so often, the more realistic situation is that the quality of opportunity dries out while you keep on pressing. In essence, you want to make sure that your aggressiveness is matching the expected value of the opportunity rather than matching the emotional state of your hot streak. With experience, you develop a deep self-awareness around your psychological tells. You need to know yourself unbelievably well in this profession because your emotional state leaks into every trading decision you make. What happens when you start overheating emotionally? For some traders, they force low-quality setups. Others impulsively increase size, and some become hyperactive and overtrade. For me personally, one of the biggest warning signs is I oversize subpar setups. Anytime I'm on a winning streak, awareness of my historical tendency is what allows me to proactively defend against that. Awareness is always the essential first step for behavioral habit change. You can even set rules for check-ins after a certain number of positive days in a row, a certain amount of P&L made in a month, or even if you have a day over a certain P&L amount. The second action traders need to do is implement hard giveback rules on their day, week, and month. When traders are on a hot streak or following a big day, most aren't even remotely thinking about losses or how vulnerable they might be. They're overeager to continue the momentum, and what they should be doing is setting safeguards. Decide proactively how much you're willing to give back before you reduce size or temporarily stop trading. Because traders become unbelievably irrational once they emotionally attach themselves to peak P&L. A trader makes $100,000 in a month, gives back $20,000, and then they stop trading the market objectively. Now they're trading their high watermark emotionally. They become obsessed with getting back to peak profits, and they're doing all this instead of evaluating whether the current opportunity environment even deserves the aggression anymore. The third proactive step is handicapping each trading day and each individual trade completely fresh. This is huge. Often times traders think they suddenly became incredible traders during a hot streak when in reality the market opportunity was simply phenomenal. Every morning after a big win or hot streak, you need to ask yourself, is today's trading environment actually as good as the previous days, or am I emotionally anchoring to my recent success? Opportunities in markets tend to cluster, then taper off. If you've ever had a really great day, or a really good week, or a really good month, the longer that continues, probabilistically, it is actually more likely that the next day will be less good than the prior days. The fourth step traders absolutely need to do during a heater is double down on best practices instead of relaxing them. Traders start winning, and suddenly the discipline disappears. Your sleep gets worse, trade reviews stop, and daily report cards, which should be an essential practice, like I described in this video, end up getting skipped. Traders stop preparing properly because success tricks them into believing they no longer need it. That is exactly backwards. During hot streaks, your daily report card becomes more important, not less. Sleep becomes more important, and emotional regulation becomes more important as well. The routines that created the success are the exact routines you need to protect yourself once your ego starts inflating. The fifth step traders should proactively do during periods of huge success is reduce outside noise and stimulation. One of the biggest mistakes traders make during heaters is turning success into identity. Suddenly, they're posting every winner online, checking social media constantly, and fantasizing about how much money they're going to make that year, mentally spending their profits before they're realized. All of that feeds into emotional instability. The more emotionally significant your P&L becomes, the harder it becomes to trade objectively. Some of the best traders I know become quieter during their biggest runs because they're trying to stay psychologically centered while everyone else starts acting like they're auditioning for Wolf of Wall Street. And finally, the sixth proactive step is remembering that survival matters more than any single hot streak. The traders who build real long-term wealth are usually not the traders who maximize every single opportunity cycle perfectly. They're the traders who are okay throttling back in the late stages of a market cycle if that means it'll help them survive enough cycles to keep compounding over decades. Avoiding catastrophic give backs is essential to trader longevity. This means staying humble while everyone else becomes euphoric. At the highest levels trading becomes less about finding setups and more about managing yourself correctly while opportunities fluctuate around you. The market environment will always change. The question is whether your psychology adapts before the market forces humility onto you. Because eventually every hot streak ends.
