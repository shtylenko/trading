residual. This single concept sits at the center of every nonlinear simulation. Whether you're solving structural deformation, fluid flow, or multifysics problems, the solver is always trying to do one thing. Eliminate the residual. Everything the nonlinear solver does is simply an attempt to drive that residual to zero. Think of the residual as a local imbalance in the physics. Imagine a small region of your computational domain. It could be a finite element, a control volume or a small patch of material. Something flows into that region and something flows out of it. If the physics is satisfied, these quantities should balance perfectly. But during the iterative solution process, they usually don't match at first. The difference between what enters and what leaves, that unbalanced remainder, is what we call the residual. And that residual tells the solver how far the current solution is from satisfying the governing equations. To formalize this idea, we write the residual mathematically. The residual R of U represents the difference between internal and external forces in the system. External forces F come from loads, boundary conditions or interactions with the environment. Internal forces represented by KU * U that arise from the material response. essentially how the structure resists deformation. If these two forces balance exactly, the residual becomes zero and the structure is in equilibrium. But if they don't match, the residual measures uh the false imbalance that still needs to be corrected. So how do nonlinear solvers remove this imbalance? They apply iterative corrections to the solution. Starting from an initial guess, the solver evaluates the residual and then adjusts the solution step by step. You can imagine this as moving across a landscape following a convergence path that gradually approaches the equilibrium point. Each iteration reduces the residual until the imbalance becomes sufficiently small. Newtonbased nonlinear solvers reduce the residual through a sequence of correction steps. First, the solver evaluates the residual which tells us how far the current solution is from equilibrium. Next, it computes the tangent, which describes how the residual changes if the displacement changes slightly. In structural mechanics, this is often called the tangent stiffness matrix. Finally, using that information, the solver calculates a correction step that updates the solution from K to K + 1. Each iteration uses the residual and the tangent to move closer to the point where the imbalance disappears and equilibrium is satisfied. In structural finite element analysis, the residual has a very physical interpretation. External loads push on the structure while the material generates internal forces that resist deformation. If these forces do not balance, the structure experiences a force imbalance. That imbalance is exactly what the residual represents and the solver continues updating the displacement field until those forces balance. In fluid simulations, the same concept appears through conservation laws. Across each control volume, the solver checks whether mass, momentum, and energy are conserved. If inflow and outflow do not match, a residual appears, indicating that conservation has not yet been satisfied. Numerical schemes then redistribute these imbalances until the flow field becomes consistent with the governing equations. So regardless of the physics, the residual always represents the gap between the current solution and the true physical balance. Nonlinear solvers exist to remove that gap.
