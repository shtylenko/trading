The first truth is that initially struggling or blowing your account does not mean you will not make it. There's this perception that elite traders just had some natural talent. They flipped a switch and they started printing money. But if you actually read the books closely, you see the opposite over and over again. Ed Seykota literally says everyone gets what they want out of the market. And what he meant by that is people unconsciously self-sabotage until they fix themselves, not their strategy. Marty Schwartz talks about struggling for years before becoming consistent. And even after success, he still had periods where he had to step back and reset. The second truth in the Market Wizards series is that risk management, above all else, is probably the most consistent theme across every single interview in those books. Paul Tudor Jones, one of the greatest goats, said it best. Do not focus on making money, focus on protecting what you have. That's not just a throwaway quote. That is the foundation of how elite traders think. Stan Druckenmiller talked about how his best trades worked because he was aggressive when he was right, but what's often missed is that he was incredibly disciplined about cutting his losses when he was wrong. Even someone like Richard Dennis, who trained the Turtle Traders, emphasized that the entire system was built around controlling losses, not just maximizing gains. It sounds cliché, but it is true. You cannot have a long successful career if you blow up. You can have a great strategy, but if you size too big, ignore your stops, or let just one trade spiral, it can be over. I've seen this first hand with so many traders that have had initial success, but just can't control their risk, and they end up blowing up. Your risk management has to hold up on your worst day, not your best day, because that's when the real test happens. Trading is one of those rare jobs where you can be incredibly talented for 999 days in a row, then one bad day can blow up everything. The traders who last are the ones who treat this like a living, breathing skill, and not just a static system.
